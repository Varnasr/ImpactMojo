Part 1: The Right to Information Act - Anatomy of Policy Success

25 minutes

üìÖ RTI Timeline: From Grassroots to National Law

1990-1996: Rajasthan experiments - MKSS (Mazdoor Kisan Shakti Sangathan) uses social audits to expose corruption in employment schemes

1997-2000: State-level laws in Rajasthan, Tamil Nadu, Goa, Delhi demonstrate feasibility

2000-2004: National advocacy coalition forms; civil society networks expand

2004: UPA government includes RTI in Common Minimum Programme

2005: RTI Act passed with strong implementation framework

üèõÔ∏è Political Champions

Sonia Gandhi (UPA), Aruna Roy (MKSS), key MPs across parties who saw electoral benefits from transparency

üì¢ Civil Society

MKSS, NCPRI, state-level RTI groups created sustained pressure and technical expertise

üì∞ Media

Consistent coverage of corruption cases; RTI successes created positive feedback loops

üè¢ Bureaucracy

Mixed responses - some progressive officers supported, others resisted; implementation varied by state

‚úÖ Why RTI Succeeded: Coalition Analysis

Broad Coalition: United urban middle class (anti-corruption), rural poor (employment rights), media (story access), opposition parties (government accountability)

Compelling Narrative: "Right to know" framed as fundamental democratic right, not just technical reform

Demonstration Effects: State-level successes proved feasibility and built momentum

Political Timing: UPA needed differentiation from NDA; corruption scandals created opening

Technical Design: Strong enforcement mechanisms; citizen-friendly procedures

Part 2: Agricultural Reforms - When Coalitions Collide

25 minutes

üåæ Case Study: 2020-21 Farm Laws - Policy Design vs. Political Reality

The Policy: Three laws aimed at liberalizing agricultural markets

  • Farmers' Produce Trade and Commerce Act (freedom to sell outside mandis)
  • Farmers' Agreement on Price Assurance Act (contract farming)
  • Essential Commodities Amendment Act (reduced stock limits)

Technical Rationale: Increase competition, improve prices, modernize supply chains, reduce government fiscal burden

‚ùå What Went Wrong

No Consultation: Laws passed without stakeholder engagement

Trust Deficit: Farmers feared loss of MSP system

Powerful Opposition Coalition: Punjab/Haryana farmers, opposition parties, some farmer unions

Symbolic Politics: Framed as attack on farmer livelihoods

Implementation Rush: No pilots or gradual rollout

‚úÖ Lessons Learned

Stakeholder Buy-in: Reform coalitions need affected groups as partners, not targets

Sequencing Matters: Build trust through smaller wins before major reforms

Federalism Dynamics: State government support crucial for agricultural policy

Compensation Design: Clear guarantees for potential losers

Political Communication: Narrative matters as much as technical content

‚öñÔ∏è Contrast: Successful Agricultural Reforms

Bihar APMC Reform (2006): Dismantled state agricultural marketing committees

  • Political Context: Nitish Kumar's development agenda; differentiation from Lalu era
  • Coalition: Progressive farmers, traders, development advocates
  • Implementation: Gradual; allowed market development over time
  • Results: Mixed but generally positive for vegetable/fruit markets

Key Difference: Bihar farmers less dependent on wheat/rice; less organized opposition; state government ownership

  2. How would you sequence reforms to build momentum?

  4. How would you address concerns of potential losers?

Part 3: Federalism and Multi-Level Governance

20 minutes

Understanding India's Complex Governance Architecture

Level Key Powers Political Dynamics Policy Implications
Central Government Defense, foreign policy, major economic policy, concurrent subjects National elections, coalition politics, party leadership Can set frameworks but needs state cooperation for implementation
State Governments Police, health, education, agriculture, state subjects State elections, regional parties, chief minister dominance Critical for service delivery; can resist or modify central policies
Local Governments Implementation of development schemes, local service delivery Local politics, party competition, social hierarchies Where policies meet citizens; variation in capacity and accountability

üèõÔ∏è Federalism in Action: GST Implementation (2017)

The Challenge: Replace multiple state and central taxes with unified Goods and Services Tax

Political Economy Factors:

  • Revenue Fears: States worried about losing tax autonomy and revenue
  • Constitutional Change: Required states to give up taxation powers
  • Technical Complexity: IT systems, rate harmonization, compliance procedures
  • Political Credit: Success/failure would affect both central and state governments

How Consensus Was Built:

  • Compensation Guarantee: 5-year revenue guarantee for states
  • GST Council: Federal decision-making body with state representation
  • Phased Implementation: Multiple rollout dates to address technical issues
  • Cross-Party Support: Opposition-ruled states included in design process

‚ö†Ô∏è When Federalism Blocks Policy: COVID-19 Response (2020-21)

The Problem: Health is a state subject, but pandemic required coordinated national response

Coordination Challenges:

  • States imposed different lockdown rules creating confusion
  • Vaccine procurement initially decentralized leading to inequities
  • Inter-state migration conflicts during lockdowns
  • Political blame-shifting between levels of government

Lesson: Crisis response requires pre-existing coordination mechanisms and clear role definitions
