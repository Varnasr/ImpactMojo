Part 1: Climate Vulnerability Assessment - Who is Most at Risk?

20 minutes

üéØ Understanding Vulnerability

IPCC Definition: Vulnerability = Exposure + Sensitivity - Adaptive Capacity

  • Exposure: Degree to which a system is in contact with climate hazards
  • Sensitivity: Degree to which a system is affected by climate stimuli
  • Adaptive Capacity: Ability to adjust to potential damages or cope with consequences

Vulnerability Patterns in South Asia

Geographic Hotspots
  • Coastal Areas: Sea level rise, cyclones, salt intrusion
  • Gangetic Plains: Flooding, heat waves, groundwater depletion
  • Western Ghats: Extreme rainfall, landslides
  • Himalayan Region: Glacial melt, flash floods
  • Arid/Semi-Arid: Droughts, desertification, heat stress
Social Vulnerability
  • Women: Higher exposure during extreme events, limited mobility
  • Children & Elderly: Health sensitivity to heat, vector-borne diseases
  • Indigenous Communities: Land-dependent livelihoods, limited access to services
  • Urban Poor: Informal settlements in risk-prone areas
  • Smallholder Farmers: Climate-dependent incomes, limited resources

üìä Case Study: Sundarbans Vulnerability

Location: Mangrove delta in West Bengal/Bangladesh border

Population: 4.4 million people across 102 islands

Part 2: Adaptation Strategies - Building Resilience

20 minutes

Adaptation Definition: Adjustments in ecological, social, or economic systems in response to actual or expected climatic stimuli and their effects, which moderates harm or exploits beneficial opportunities.

Types of Adaptation Approaches

üõ°Ô∏è Ecosystem-Based Adaptation

Principle: Use biodiversity and ecosystem services to help people adapt

Indian Examples:

  • Mangrove Restoration: Sundarbans, Gujarat coast for storm protection
  • Watershed Management: Rajasthan's water harvesting systems
  • Urban Green Infrastructure: Sponge cities, green roofs in Pune
  • Coral Reef Conservation: Lakshadweep, Andaman islands

üèóÔ∏è Infrastructure-Based Adaptation

Principle: Build physical systems to reduce climate risks

Indian Examples:

  • Early Warning Systems: Cyclone tracking, heat wave alerts
  • Climate-Resilient Housing: Disaster-resistant construction
  • Flood Management: Chennai's flood control systems
  • Drought-Proofing: Drip irrigation, water storage

üåæ Community-Based Adaptation

Principle: Build local capacity and use traditional knowledge

Indian Examples:

  • Climate-Smart Agriculture: SRI rice cultivation, crop diversification
  • Traditional Water Management: Tank systems in Tamil Nadu
  • Livelihood Diversification: Agriculture + aquaculture + livestock
  • Community Seed Banks: Preserving climate-resilient varieties

üìã Policy-Based Adaptation

Principle: Create enabling environments for adaptation

Indian Examples:

  • National Action Plan on Climate Change (NAPCC): 8 national missions
  • State Action Plans: Context-specific strategies
  • Climate Risk Insurance: Weather-based crop insurance
  • Disaster Management Acts: Legal frameworks for resilience

üåæ Success Story: Maharashtra's Climate-Resilient Villages

Challenge: Recurring droughts affecting 1000+ villages in Marathwada region

Intervention: Integrated approach combining multiple adaptation strategies

Key Components:

  • Water Security: Watershed treatment, check dams, farm ponds (30% increase in groundwater)
  • Climate-Smart Agriculture: Drought-tolerant seeds, drip irrigation (40% water savings)
  • Livelihood Diversification: Dairy, poultry, kitchen gardens (25% income increase)
  • Institutional Strengthening: Village climate committees, weather-based advisories

Results (2019-2024): 300+ villages achieved drought resilience, reduced distress migration by 60%

Part 3: Mitigation Options - Reducing Emissions

15 minutes

Mitigation Definition: Human interventions to reduce greenhouse gas emissions or enhance sinks that remove them from the atmosphere.

India's Mitigation Landscape

‚ö° Energy Sector Transformation

Current Status: India has 4th largest renewable energy capacity globally (180 GW)

Targets: 500 GW renewable energy by 2030, Net Zero by 2070

Key Initiatives:

  • Solar Mission: 100 GW solar capacity target (achieved 75 GW by 2024)
  • Wind Power: 140 GW potential, rapid offshore development
  • Green Hydrogen: National mission for clean fuel production
  • Energy Efficiency: LED distribution, building codes, industrial standards

Co-benefits: Energy security, air quality improvement, job creation (3.3 million renewable energy jobs)

üöå Transport & Urban Planning

Challenge: Transport contributes 12% of India's emissions, growing at 6% annually

Mitigation Options:

  • Electric Mobility: FAME scheme, charging infrastructure (2 million EVs target by 2026)
  • Public Transit: Metro expansion, BRT systems, cycling infrastructure
  • Freight Efficiency: Dedicated freight corridors, logistics optimization
  • Urban Planning: Compact development, mixed-use zoning, smart cities

Co-benefits: Air quality, traffic congestion, public health, urban livability

Potential: Forests and land use can provide 30% of needed emission reductions

Part 4: Climate Justice - Ensuring Equitable Action

15 minutes

‚öñÔ∏è Climate Justice Principles

Distributive Justice: Fair distribution of benefits and burdens of climate action

Procedural Justice: Meaningful participation in climate decision-making

Recognition Justice: Acknowledgment of different values, knowledge systems, and rights

Corrective Justice: Accountability for climate harms and appropriate remedies

Justice Challenges in Indian Climate Action

Renewable Energy Projects

Benefits: Clean energy, reduced emissions

Justice Concerns:

  • Land acquisition affects farming communities
  • Benefits accrue to urban consumers
  • Limited local employment generation
  • Inadequate consultation processes
Forest Conservation

Benefits: Carbon sequestration, biodiversity

Justice Concerns:

  • Restrictions on traditional forest use
  • Limited recognition of forest rights
  • Displacement of indigenous communities
  • Top-down conservation approaches

‚öñÔ∏è Climate Justice Analysis (10 minutes)

Case Study: Large-scale solar park development in Rajasthan

Project Details: 5,000 hectares of agricultural land acquired for 750 MW solar park

Benefits: Clean energy generation, reduced coal dependence, state revenue

Costs: 2,000 farming families displaced, loss of traditional livelihoods, cultural disruption

Justice Assessment Framework (6 minutes):

  1. Distributive Justice: Who bears the costs vs. who receives the benefits? Is this fair?
  2. Procedural Justice: Were affected communities meaningfully consulted? Did they have decision-making power?
  3. Recognition Justice: Were traditional land rights and farming knowledge valued? Cultural impacts considered?
  4. Corrective Justice: What compensation was provided? Are there grievance mechanisms?

Redesign Challenge (4 minutes): How could this project be redesigned to be more just? What alternative approaches exist?

üîÑ Principles for Just Climate Action

  • Free, Prior, Informed Consent: Genuine community participation in all phases
  • Benefit Sharing: Local communities receive fair share of project benefits
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Respect for traditional knowledge and practices
  • Adaptive Management: Flexibility to address emerging concerns
  • Accountability Mechanisms: Independent monitoring and grievance systems

Key Takeaway

Effective climate action requires understanding vulnerability patterns, designing context-appropriate solutions, and ensuring that climate interventions advance justice rather than perpetuate inequalities. Science must be coupled with equity.
