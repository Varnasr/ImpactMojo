Part 1: Foundations - Sex, Gender, and Sexuality

20 minutes

Opening Scenario: The Story of Arjun and Priya

Arjun (age 8) loves dancing and wants to learn Bharatanatyam. His family says "boys don't dance" and enrolls him in cricket instead.

Priya (age 8) excels at math and wants to be an engineer. Her family worries that being "too smart" will make marriage difficult.

What assumptions about gender are operating here? How do these early messages shape life possibilities?

Core Concepts: Building Our Vocabulary

Concept Definition Example from South Asian Context
Sex Biological characteristics (chromosomes, hormones, anatomy) Male, female, intersex variations
Gender Social roles, behaviors, expressions society associates with sex Indian women expected to be nurturing; men expected to be providers
Gender Identity Internal sense of being male, female, both, or neither Hijra communities, transgender experiences, non-binary identities
Gender Expression How one presents gender through clothing, behavior, voice Saris vs. kurtas, mehendi vs. beards, speech patterns
Sexual Orientation Romantic/sexual attraction patterns Heterosexual, lesbian, gay, bisexual, asexual, pansexual

Key Insight: Gender is not binary or fixed. It's a spectrum of identities, expressions, and experiences that vary across cultures and individuals. What we consider "natural" gender roles are often learned social expectations.

üé≠ Activity: Gender Role Analysis (8 minutes)

Work in pairs. Each pair gets one scenario:

Scenario A: Family planning - who makes decisions about having children?

Scenario B: Household chores - who does cooking, cleaning, childcare?

Scenario C: Career choices - which jobs are "suitable" for women vs men?

Part 2: Feminist Theory - Frameworks for Understanding Power

20 minutes

Major Feminist Waves and Approaches

üåä First Wave Feminism (1850s-1920s)

Focus: Legal rights, suffrage, property rights

South Asian Connection: Women's education movements, social reform (Savitribai Phule, Pandita Ramabai)

Key Achievement: Women's right to vote, inheritance rights

Limitation: Primarily addressed privileged women's concerns

üåä Second Wave Feminism (1960s-1980s)

Focus: Workplace equality, reproductive rights, domestic violence

South Asian Connection: Chipko movement, anti-dowry campaigns, women's development programs

Key Achievement: Recognition of unpaid care work, violence against women

Limitation: Often centered experiences of educated, urban women

üåä Third Wave Feminism (1990s-2000s)

Focus: Intersectionality, diversity, individual choice

South Asian Connection: Dalit feminism, queer movements, global feminism critiques

Key Achievement: Recognition of multiple identities and experiences

Current Evolution: Digital feminism, #MeToo, trans-inclusive feminism

Key Feminist Concepts

üîç Patriarchy

Definition: A system where men hold primary power in political, social, and economic spheres

How it operates in South Asia:

  • Family structures: Joint families with male heads, son preference
  • Economic control: Women's labor undervalued, property inheritance patterns
  • Political representation: Low women's representation in politics, decision-making
  • Cultural norms: Honor concepts, women's mobility restrictions, dress codes

‚öñÔ∏è Gender and Power

Three Dimensions of Power (Rowlands Framework):

  • Power Over: Domination, control (patriarchal power)
  • Power To: Ability to act, make decisions (individual agency)
  • Power With: Collective action, solidarity (women's movements)
  • Power Within: Self-confidence, dignity, internal strength

Part 3: Intersectionality - Understanding Multiple Identities

15 minutes

Intersectionality (Kimberl√© Crenshaw): People have multiple, overlapping identities that create unique experiences of privilege and oppression. Gender never exists in isolation from race, class, caste, religion, sexuality, disability, and other identities.

Intersectionality in South Asian Contexts

Caste + Gender

Dalit women face discrimination both as women and as Dalits, experiencing unique forms of violence and exclusion

Class + Gender

Upper-class women may have economic resources but still face mobility restrictions; working-class women face both economic and gender constraints

Religion + Gender

Muslim women in India navigate both Islamophobia and gender discrimination; religious minority women face unique challenges

Sexuality + Gender

LGBTQIA+ individuals face discrimination for both gender identity/expression and sexual orientation

Region + Gender

Women from Northeast India face both racism and gender discrimination in other parts of India

Disability + Gender

Women with disabilities face ableism and gender discrimination, often excluded from both women's and disability movements

Intersectional Analysis: Three Women's Stories

Lakshmi: Upper-caste, educated, urban software engineer. Faces workplace sexism but has family support and economic independence.

Fatima: Muslim, working-class, small town. Faces religious discrimination, economic pressure, and restrictions on mobility.

Kavitha: Dalit, rural, disabled. Experiences caste discrimination, gender bias, ableism, and economic marginalization.

How do their experiences of gender inequality differ? What different strategies and supports do they need?

Scenario: You're designing a women's economic empowerment program in West Bengal

Challenge: How do you ensure your program reaches and benefits:

  • Women from different caste backgrounds
  • Hindu and Muslim women
  • Women with and without formal education
  • Women with disabilities
  • Single, married, and widowed women

  3. What different types of support might different groups need?
  4. How would you measure success differently for different groups?

Part 4: LGBTQIA+ Identities and Queer Movements in India

15 minutes

Understanding LGBTQIA+ Identities

üè≥Ô∏è‚Äçüåà LGBTQIA+ Breakdown

  • Lesbian: Women attracted to women
  • Gay: Men attracted to men (also used as umbrella term)
  • Bisexual: Attracted to more than one gender
  • Transgender: Gender identity differs from assigned sex at birth
  • Queer/Questioning: Umbrella term or exploring identity
  • Intersex: Born with reproductive/sexual anatomy that doesn't fit typical definitions
  • Asexual: Little or no sexual attraction
  • +: Includes other identities (pansexual, non-binary, etc.)

Historical and Cultural Context in South Asia

‚öñÔ∏è Legal Milestones in India

  • 2009: Delhi High Court decriminalizes homosexuality
  • 2013: Supreme Court reverses decision, reinstates Section 377
  • 2014: NALSA judgment recognizes transgender rights
  • 2018: Section 377 struck down; homosexuality decriminalized
  • 2019: Transgender Persons Act (controversial provisions)

üèõÔ∏è Traditional Third Gender Recognition

South Asia has long histories of gender diversity:

  • Hijras: Traditional third gender community with cultural and religious roles
  • Aravanis (Tamil Nadu): Transgender women community
  • Kothi/Panthi: Terms for gender expression in gay communities
  • Historical evidence: Khajuraho sculptures, Mahabharata stories, Mughal court traditions

Contemporary Challenges and Movements

Challenge Impact Movement Response
Family rejection Homelessness, mental health issues, economic vulnerability Support groups, chosen families, advocacy for family acceptance
Workplace discrimination Limited job opportunities, harassment, income insecurity Corporate diversity initiatives, legal advocacy, skill-building programs
Healthcare barriers Discrimination by providers, lack of appropriate services Healthcare advocacy, provider training, community health initiatives
Violence and harassment Physical safety concerns, police harassment Legal literacy, safety networks, police sensitization

Part 5: Applying Gender Analysis to Development Work

5 minutes

Gender Analysis Framework for Programs

üîç Key Questions for Any Development Initiative

  1. Who participates? Who is included/excluded? Who makes decisions?
  2. Who benefits? How are benefits distributed? Who controls resources?
  3. Who bears costs? What are the risks and burdens for different groups?
  4. How are power relations affected? Does the intervention challenge or reinforce inequalities?

üéØ Action Planning: Gender Lens in Your Work (5 minutes)

Quick Self-Assessment:

  1. Current Practice: How do you currently consider gender in your work?
  2. One Change: What's one specific way you could apply a stronger gender lens?
  3. Next Step: What will you do differently in your next project or program?

Key Takeaway

Gender is not about women only - it's about understanding how power, identity, and social structures shape all our lives. True gender equality requires transforming systems, not just adding women to existing structures.
