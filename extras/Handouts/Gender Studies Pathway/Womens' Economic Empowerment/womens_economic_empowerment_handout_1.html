Part 1: Defining Women's Economic Empowerment

20 minutes

üë©‚Äçüíº Two Women, Different Stories

Priya's Story: Works 12 hours daily in a garment factory earning ‚Çπ8,000/month. Husband takes her salary. Has no savings account. Cannot visit family without permission. Dreams of starting a tailoring business but has no access to credit or business training.

Sunita's Story: Runs a successful catering business earning ‚Çπ25,000/month. Controls her income and business decisions. Owns her equipment and has a business bank account. Participates in local women's business network. Recently expanded to hire 3 other women.

The Question: Both women are economically active. Which one is economically empowered? What makes the difference?

Participation vs. Empowerment

Economic Participation

  • Focus: Getting women into economic activities
  • Metrics: Employment rates, labor force participation
  • Approach: Skills training, job creation, microfinance
  • Question: "Are women working and earning?"
  • Example: Priya - employed but powerless

Economic Empowerment

  • Focus: Enabling women's control over economic choices
  • Metrics: Decision-making power, asset ownership, agency
  • Approach: Addressing structural barriers, changing norms
  • Question: "Do women control their economic lives?"
  • Example: Sunita - autonomous economic agent

‚ö†Ô∏è The Empowerment Paradox: Increased economic participation doesn't automatically lead to empowerment. Women can be economically active but still lack control over resources, decisions, or their own mobility. True empowerment requires addressing power relations, not just providing economic opportunities.

Defining Economic Empowerment

üéØ The Three Dimensions Framework (Kabeer, 1999)

Economic empowerment occurs when women have the ability to succeed and advance economically and have the power to make and act on economic decisions.

üí∞ Resources

Access to economic assets and opportunities

Examples: Land, credit, savings, skills, social networks, time

Barriers: Discriminatory laws, collateral requirements, social norms

‚ö° Agency

Ability to make choices and act on them

Examples: Decision-making power, negotiation skills, leadership, voice

Barriers: Gender norms, lack of confidence, family restrictions

üéØ Achievements

Outcomes that reflect empowered choices

Examples: Increased income, asset accumulation, status, well-being

Barriers: Market discrimination, unpaid care work, social backlash

Key Insight: All three dimensions must work together. Resources without agency = dependency. Agency without resources = frustration. Achievements without sustained resources and agency = temporary gains.

Part 2: Institutional Barriers to Economic Empowerment

25 minutes

Understanding Structural Constraints

Women's economic empowerment is constrained by multiple, intersecting institutional barriers that operate at different levels of society.

üö´ Five Categories of Institutional Barriers
Barrier Type Examples in Indian Context Impact on Empowerment
Legal & Regulatory Hindu succession laws, property registration processes, labor law exclusions Limits asset ownership, formal employment access
Financial System Collateral requirements, male guarantor needs, banking hour restrictions Excludes women from formal credit and savings
Market Structures Male-dominated value chains, discriminatory pricing, limited mobility Confines women to low-value economic activities
Social Norms Son preference, purdah systems, gendered division of labor Restricts choices and reinforces economic dependence
Care Economy Unpaid domestic work, elderly care responsibilities, lack of childcare Creates "time poverty" limiting economic opportunities
üáÆüá≥ Indian Context: The Numbers Tell the Story

Key Statistics on Women's Economic Status:

  • Labor Force Participation: Only 25% of women vs. 79% of men (PLFS 2019-20)
  • Land ownership: Women own only 12.8% of agricultural land
  • Financial inclusion: 78% women have bank accounts but only 35% actively use them
  • Wage gap: Women earn 34% less than men for similar work
  • Unpaid work: Women spend 5 hours daily on unpaid care vs. men's 1.6 hours
  • Entrepreneurship: Women own only 14% of MSMEs despite constituting 50% of population

Part 3: Measuring Economic Empowerment

20 minutes

Beyond Simple Metrics

Traditional economic measures miss the empowerment dimension. We need multidimensional approaches that capture agency, not just outcomes.

üè¶ Financial Inclusion Indicators

Basic: Bank account ownership, credit access

Empowerment: Control over savings, decision-making on financial services use

Question: Who decides how money is used?

üíº Employment Indicators

Basic: Employment rates, income levels

Empowerment: Job choice autonomy, control over earnings, career advancement

Question: Can women choose their work and keep their earnings?

üè¢ Entrepreneurship Indicators

Basic: Business ownership, revenue generation

Empowerment: Business decision-making, asset control, growth autonomy

Question: Who controls the business and its profits?

üìè The Women's Empowerment in Agriculture Index (WEAI)

Developed by: USAID, IFPRI, Oxford Poverty & Human Development Initiative

Five Domains of Empowerment:

  1. Production decisions: Input in productive decisions
  2. Resource ownership: Asset ownership and control
  3. Income control: Control over use of income
  4. Leadership: Group membership and speaking in public
  5. Time allocation: Satisfaction with time for leisure activities

Key Innovation: Measures adequacy (percentage achieving empowerment) and parity (gap between women and men in same household)

Practical Measurement Approaches

Measurement Approach Best For Key Questions Limitations
Decision-making surveys Household-level programs "Who usually decides about...?" Social desirability bias
Asset ownership tracking Asset-building programs "Who has legal ownership/control?" Formal vs. informal ownership
Time-use diaries Understanding constraints "How do women spend their day?" Labor-intensive data collection
Financial behavior tracking Financial inclusion programs "Who controls financial decisions?" Privacy and access issues
Qualitative empowerment stories Understanding mechanisms "How has your life changed?" Hard to aggregate or compare

Part 4: Designing for Empowerment

10 minutes

Program Design Principles

üéØ Address Root Causes

Don't just provide opportunities - change the systems that limit women's choices

Example: Combine credit with legal literacy and collective action

üè† Work at Multiple Levels

Individual, household, community, and institutional change simultaneously

Example: Skills training + men's engagement + policy advocacy

‚è∞ Recognize Time Constraints

Address unpaid care work that limits women's economic participation

Example: Childcare provision, time-saving technologies

ü§ù Build Collective Agency

Individual empowerment is strengthened through group solidarity and action

Example: Women's groups, collective enterprises, advocacy networks

üìè Measure What Matters

Track empowerment indicators, not just economic outcomes

Example: Decision-making power, asset control, life satisfaction

üîÑ Adapt and Iterate

Empowerment is context-specific and requires continuous learning

Example: Regular feedback loops, participatory evaluation

üéØ Key Takeaway

Women's economic empowerment is not just about getting women into the economy - it's about transforming the economy to work for women. True empowerment requires addressing structural barriers that limit women's agency and control over their economic lives.
