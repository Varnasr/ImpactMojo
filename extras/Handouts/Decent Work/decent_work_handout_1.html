Part 1: What is Decent Work? - Beyond Just Having a Job

💼 Three Workers, Three Realities: The Decent Work Spectrum

Rajesh (Construction Worker, Delhi):

  • Daily wage: ₹400-500 (when work available)
  • Works 6 days/week, 10-12 hours/day
  • No written contract, paid daily in cash
  • No safety equipment, frequent injuries
  • No health insurance, EPF, or paid leave
  • Job security: None - hired daily

Priya (Software Engineer, Bangalore):

  • Monthly salary: ₹80,000
  • Works 45-50 hours/week (sometimes more during deadlines)
  • Written contract, annual increments
  • Health insurance, PF, stock options
  • Paid vacation, maternity leave
  • High stress, work-life balance issues

Sunita (Domestic Worker, Mumbai):

  • Monthly income: ₹12,000 (4 houses)
  • Works 8-10 hours/day, 7 days/week
  • Verbal agreements, irregular payments
  • Respect and dignity issues
  • No social security, benefits
  • Vulnerable to sudden termination

All three have "jobs" but vastly different work experiences. Decent work is about quality, not just quantity of employment.

The ILO Decent Work Framework

Definition: Decent work is productive work for women and men in conditions of freedom, equity, security and human dignity.

Goal: All people should have opportunities for work that is:

  • Productive and delivers a fair income
  • Provides security in the workplace and social protection for families
  • Offers better prospects for personal development and social integration
  • Gives people freedom to express their concerns, organize and participate in decisions affecting their lives
  • Guarantees equal opportunities and treatment for all women and men

The Four Pillars of Decent Work

🏭 Employment Creation

Focus: Creating jobs and economic opportunities

  • Employment-to-population ratios
  • Unemployment rates
  • Informal employment shares
  • Skills development programs
⚖️ Rights at Work

Focus: Fundamental principles and rights

  • Freedom of association
  • Collective bargaining rights
  • Elimination of forced labor
  • Abolition of child labor
  • Non-discrimination at work
🛡️ Social Protection

Focus: Security and safety nets

  • Social security coverage
  • Occupational safety and health
  • Maternity protection
  • Pensions and disability benefits
  • Unemployment protection
🤝 Social Dialogue

Focus: Tripartite cooperation

  • Worker representation
  • Employer organization
  • Government facilitation
  • Collective bargaining
  • Dispute resolution mechanisms

Part 3: Indian Labor Market Context

20 minutes

Structural Challenges to Decent Work in India

❌ Current Challenges

  • High Informality: 90% workers lack formal contracts
  • Low Productivity: Agricultural employment at 46% but 18% of GDP
  • Gender Gaps: Women's LFPR dropped to 25% (2017-18)
  • Skill Mismatches: 68% firms report skill shortages
  • Weak Social Protection: Only 10% have comprehensive coverage
  • Poor Working Conditions: Limited OSH enforcement

✅ Reform Opportunities

  • Demographic Dividend: 65% under 35 years old
  • Digital Economy: Growing platform and gig work
  • Policy Momentum: New labor codes, social security
  • Skill India Mission: Large-scale capacity building
  • Financial Inclusion: JAM trinity enabling direct transfers
  • Manufacturing Growth: Make in India, PLI schemes

India's New Labor Code Framework

📋 Four New Labor Codes (2019-2020)

1. Code on Wages (2019):

  • Universal minimum wage floor
  • Timely payment provisions
  • Gender-neutral wage policies

2. Industrial Relations Code (2020):

  • Ease of hiring and firing
  • Threshold for standing orders raised to 300
  • Re-skilling fund provisions

3. Social Security Code (2020):

  • Extended coverage to gig and platform workers
  • Portable social security accounts
  • Aggregator liability for social security

4. Occupational Safety and Health Code (2020):

  • Unified OSH framework
  • Extended coverage to all establishments
  • Women's night work provisions

Decent Work Assessment: Progress on formalization and social protection, but concerns about weakened worker protections and collective bargaining rights.

Part 4: Policy Analysis Exercise

20 minutes

🏛️ Policy Evaluation: MGNREGA and Decent Work

Background: MGNREGA guarantees 100 days of wage employment annually to rural households. Analyze its performance using the decent work framework.

MGNREGA Performance Data (2022-23): • Households provided employment: 60.2 million • Person-days of employment: 2.73 billion • Average days per household: 45.3 • Average wage rate: ₹202 per day • Total expenditure: ₹89,000 crores • Wage expenditure: 60% of total • Women's participation: 53% • SC/ST participation: 59% • Completion rate of works: 82%
Decent Work Assessment Framework (15 minutes):

Pillar 1: Employment Creation

Questions to Analyze:

  • How many rural households benefit from MGNREGA employment? What % of rural workforce?
  • Is the 45.3 days average adequate for household income security?
  • Calculate: If minimum 100 days were provided to all enrolled households, what would be the additional cost?

Your Assessment: _____ (Rate 1-5 and justify)

Pillar 2: Rights at Work

Evaluation Criteria:

  • Legal guarantee vs. on-demand employment
  • Transparency through social audits
  • Grievance redressal mechanisms
  • Non-discrimination in job allocation

Your Assessment: _____ (Rate 1-5 and justify)

Pillar 3: Social Protection

Analysis Points:

  • Wage rates vs. minimum wages in states
  • Timely payment (within 15 days mandate)
  • Work site facilities (water, shade, first aid)
  • Accident compensation provisions

Your Assessment: _____ (Rate 1-5 and justify)

Pillar 4: Social Dialogue

Mechanisms to Evaluate:

  • Gram Sabha participation in planning
  • Worker committees and vigilance groups
  • Social audit processes
  • Complaint and feedback systems

Your Assessment: _____ (Rate 1-5 and justify)

Overall Decent Work Score: _____/20

Policy Recommendations: Based on your analysis, what are the top 3 improvements needed to make MGNREGA more aligned with decent work principles?

Key Takeaway

Decent work is about quality, not just quantity of employment. Job creation without attention to wages, working conditions, rights, and social protection can perpetuate poverty and inequality rather than promoting development.
