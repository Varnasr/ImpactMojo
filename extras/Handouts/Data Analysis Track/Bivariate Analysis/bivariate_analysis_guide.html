What is Bivariate Analysis in Development Context?

Definition: Statistical analysis of relationships between two variables to understand how development indicators relate to each other in South Asian contexts - such as education and income, distance and health access, or policy interventions and outcomes.

Why Essential: Most development questions involve understanding how one factor influences another. Bivariate analysis helps identify these relationships before moving to more complex multivariate models.

South Asian Development Context

  • Rural-urban relationships: How distance affects service access
  • Gender and outcomes: How women's education relates to child health
  • Policy evaluation: Does program participation improve outcomes?
  • Economic relationships: Income and consumption patterns

CORRELATION ANALYSIS

Purpose: Measure strength and direction of linear relationships between continuous variables

Pearson Correlation Coefficient (r)
Range: -1 to +1
r = 0: No linear relationship
r = +1: Perfect positive relationship
r = -1: Perfect negative relationship

Interpretation Guide:

  • |r| < 0.3: Weak relationship
  • 0.3 ≤ |r| < 0.7: Moderate relationship
  • |r| ≥ 0.7: Strong relationship

Key Assumptions:

  • Linearity: Relationship should be linear
  • Normality: Both variables should be normally distributed
  • Independence: Observations should be independent
  • Homoscedasticity: Equal variance across the range

Important Limitations:

  • Correlation ≠ Causation: Strong correlation doesn't prove causation
  • Outlier sensitivity: Extreme values can distort correlation
  • Linear only: May miss curved relationships

SIMPLE LINEAR REGRESSION

Purpose: Model the relationship between variables and make predictions

Simple Linear Regression: Y = β₀ + β₁X + ε
β₀ = Intercept (Y when X = 0)
β₁ = Slope (change in Y per unit change in X)
ε = Error term

Key Components:

  • R²: Proportion of variance explained by the model
  • p-value: Statistical significance of the relationship
  • Confidence intervals: Range of plausible values for coefficients

Data Quality and Assumptions

Common Issues in South Asian Development Data

Missing Data Patterns

  • Not random: Often reflects social exclusion or access barriers
  • Gender bias: Women's data may be missing or reported by male household heads
  • Seasonal migration: Affects data collection timing
  • Rural-urban differences: Different data collection challenges

Measurement Challenges

  • Income reporting: Informal economy income hard to measure
  • Social desirability bias: Responses influenced by perceived "correct" answers
  • Unit conversions: Different units across regions (local measures vs. standard)
  • Time periods: Agricultural vs. calendar years

Assumption Checking Strategies

Visual Methods:

  • Scatterplots: Check linearity, outliers, homoscedasticity
  • Histograms: Check normality of variables
  • Box plots: Compare distributions across groups
  • Residual plots: Check regression assumptions

Statistical Tests:

  • Shapiro-Wilk test: Test normality (small samples)
  • Levene's test: Test equal variances
  • Durbin-Watson test: Test independence of residuals

Reporting and Communication

Presenting Results to Different Audiences

For Policymakers:

  • Focus on practical significance, not just statistical significance
  • Use clear language: "For every additional year of education, malnutrition decreases by 2.3 percentage points"
  • Include confidence intervals: "The effect is between 1.8 and 2.8 percentage points"
  • Visual: Simple scatter plot with trend line

For Community Members:

  • Use local examples and familiar units
  • Avoid statistical jargon
  • Focus on what the relationship means for their lives
  • Visual: Bar charts comparing groups or simple before/after comparisons

For Researchers:

  • Include all statistical details: coefficients, p-values, confidence intervals
  • Report assumption checks and any violations
  • Discuss limitations and alternative explanations
  • Visual: Complete regression diagnostics

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Statistical Mistakes

  • Correlation ≠ Causation: Don't imply causation from correlation alone
  • Ignoring assumptions: Check linearity, normality, independence
  • Sample size issues: Too small samples give unreliable results
  • Cherry-picking: Don't select only significant results to report

Contextual Mistakes

  • Ignoring cultural factors: Statistical relationships may have cultural explanations
  • Urban bias: Applying urban-derived relationships to rural contexts
  • Time effects: Relationships may change over seasons or years
  • Aggregation issues: Individual vs. household vs. community level relationships
