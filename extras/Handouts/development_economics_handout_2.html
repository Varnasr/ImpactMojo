Part 1: Institutions and Economic Development

25 minutes

Understanding Institutional Quality

Key Concept: Institutions are the "rules of the game" - formal laws, regulations, and informal norms that shape economic behavior. Quality institutions provide predictability, enforce contracts, protect property rights, and limit corruption.

üèõÔ∏è Case Study: Land Titling in India

The Problem: Only 35% of rural land in India has clear titles, creating uncertainty for farmers and limiting access to credit.

Institutional Analysis:

  • Formal Rules: Land Revenue Acts, Registration Acts vary by state
  • Informal Norms: Traditional inheritance patterns, caste-based land access
  • Enforcement: Weak patwari system, corruption in land records
  • Political Economy: Elite capture, resistance to computerization

Economic Impact:

  • Agricultural productivity 23% lower on untitled land
  • Credit access reduced by 60% without clear titles
  • Land market transactions 40% lower in areas with poor records

Part 2: State Capacity and Governance

20 minutes

Measuring and Building State Capacity

State Capacity: The ability of government to effectively implement policy, deliver services, maintain order, and respond to citizen needs. It encompasses administrative capacity, fiscal capacity, and legal capacity.

üìä State Capacity Framework

Capacity Dimension Measurement Indicators India Performance Key Challenges
Administrative Civil service effectiveness, digitization, service delivery Government Effectiveness: 56th percentile Bureaucratic delays, skill gaps, coordination issues
Fiscal Tax collection efficiency, budget execution, fiscal balance Tax-to-GDP: 11.7% (vs 15% global average) Large informal sector, low direct tax compliance
Legal Court efficiency, contract enforcement, dispute resolution Case clearance rate: 89% (backlog growing) Judicial vacancies, procedural delays, access barriers
Coercive Police effectiveness, crime rates, conflict management Police per 100k: 150 (vs 300 UN standard) Police shortages, inadequate training, political interference

üöÄ Case Study: Digital India and State Capacity

The Initiative: Digital transformation of government services through technology platforms

State Capacity Building Elements:

  • Digital Infrastructure: JAM Trinity (Jan Dhan-Aadhaar-Mobile), broadband expansion
  • Service Delivery: Online portals, mobile apps, common service centers
  • Administrative Reform: e-governance, digital signatures, paperless offices
  • Financial Inclusion: Direct benefit transfers, digital payments

Capacity Enhancement Results:

  • Service delivery time reduced by 50-80% for many services
  • Corruption in benefit distribution reduced significantly
  • Administrative costs decreased by 25-40%
  • Financial inclusion increased from 53% to 80% of adults
State Capacity Index Calculation:
Administrative Capacity = (Service Delivery Score √ó 0.4) + (Digitization Index √ó 0.3) + (Civil Service Quality √ó 0.3)
Fiscal Capacity = (Tax Effort Ratio √ó 0.5) + (Budget Execution Rate √ó 0.3) + (Debt Sustainability √ó 0.2)
Legal Capacity = (Court Efficiency √ó 0.4) + (Contract Enforcement √ó 0.3) + (Access to Justice √ó 0.3)
Overall State Capacity = (Admin √ó 0.4) + (Fiscal √ó 0.35) + (Legal √ó 0.25)

Part 3: Policy Evaluation Methods

20 minutes

From Policy Design to Impact Assessment

üìà Policy Evaluation Framework

Evaluation Level Key Questions Methods South Asian Example
Input Evaluation Are resources adequate and well-allocated? Budget analysis, resource mapping NREGA fund utilization rates by state
Process Evaluation Is implementation happening as designed? Monitoring data, process tracking NREGA work completion rates, delay analysis
Output Evaluation Are intended outputs being delivered? Service statistics, administrative data Number of NREGA work days provided
Outcome Evaluation Are outcomes improving for beneficiaries? Before-after, treatment-control designs Income effects, asset accumulation from NREGA
Impact Evaluation What changes are attributable to the policy? RCTs, quasi-experimental methods Causal impact of NREGA on poverty reduction

üçΩÔ∏è Case Study: Midday Meal Scheme Evaluation

Policy: Provide cooked meals to 120 million school children to improve nutrition and education outcomes

Evaluation Approach:

  • Input Analysis: ‚Çπ13,215 crore allocated (2021-22), cost per meal: ‚Çπ6.30
  • Process Monitoring: 95% schools participating, quality audits, grievance mechanisms
  • Output Measurement: 11.8 crore children receiving meals daily
  • Outcome Assessment: Nutrition status, school attendance, learning outcomes
  • Impact Estimation: Randomized evaluation shows 15% increase in enrollment

Mixed Results:

  • Positive: Increased enrollment and attendance, especially for girls
  • Mixed: Nutrition improvements vary by state implementation quality
  • Challenges: Infrastructure gaps, quality concerns, caste discrimination
üî¨ Experimental Methods

Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs)

Strengths: Gold standard for causal inference, high internal validity

Limitations: Expensive, ethical concerns, external validity questions

Example: Teacher incentive schemes in Andhra Pradesh

üìä Quasi-Experimental Methods

Difference-in-Differences, IV, RDD

Strengths: Use existing variation, cost-effective, policy-relevant

Limitations: Require strong assumptions, complex interpretation

Example: Bank branch expansion impact on economic growth

Part 4: Applied Problem-Solving

15 minutes

Institutional Design Challenge

üéØ Challenge: Designing Anti-Corruption Institutions

Scenario: You're on a committee to design new anti-corruption institutions for a state government experiencing high levels of petty corruption in public services.

Current Situation:

  • Citizens report paying bribes for 65% of public service interactions
  • Average bribe amount: ‚Çπ450 per transaction
  • Low trust in existing complaint mechanisms (12% satisfaction)
  • Weak enforcement: Only 2% of complaints lead to action

Your Task (12 minutes):

Step 1: Institutional Analysis (3 minutes)

  • What institutional factors enable corruption?
  • Where are the key intervention points?

Step 2: Design Framework (4 minutes)

  • What institutions would you create/reform?
  • How would they interact with existing systems?

Step 3: Implementation Strategy (3 minutes)

  • What are the political economy constraints?
  • How would you build support for reform?

Step 4: Evaluation Plan (2 minutes)

  • How would you measure success?
  • What indicators would show institutional change?

Group Discussion (3 minutes): Share designs and discuss feasibility

üåü Key Takeaway

Economic development is fundamentally about building institutions that work for people. Strong institutions don't guarantee development, but weak institutions almost certainly prevent it.
