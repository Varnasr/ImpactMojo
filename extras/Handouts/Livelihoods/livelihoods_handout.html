Defining Livelihoods

🎯 What is a Livelihood?

DFID Definition: "A livelihood comprises the capabilities, assets, and activities required for a means of living. A livelihood is sustainable when it can cope with and recover from stress and shocks, maintain or enhance its capabilities and assets, and provide sustainable livelihood opportunities for the next generation."

Key Components:

  • Capabilities: Skills, knowledge, health, ability to work
  • Assets: Resources people can use to build livelihoods
  • Activities: How people use assets to generate income/wellbeing
  • Institutions: Rules, customs, organizations that shape access
  • Outcomes: What people achieve through their livelihood strategies

🌱 Natural Capital

What: Natural resources and environmental services

Examples: Land, water, forests, biodiversity, clean air

💰 Financial Capital

What: Financial resources available for livelihood activities

Examples: Cash, savings, credit, insurance, remittances

Indian Context: Limited formal credit, high informality

🏗️ Physical Capital

What: Infrastructure and tools that support livelihoods

Examples: Roads, buildings, equipment, technology, utilities

Indian Context: Infrastructure gaps, digital divide

🤝 Social Capital

What: Social networks, relationships, and institutions

Examples: Family, community groups, caste networks, SHGs

Indian Context: Strong family ties, caste influences, gender barriers

Part 2: Rural and Urban Livelihood Dynamics

20 minutes

Rural Livelihood Patterns

🌾 Characteristics of Rural Livelihoods in India

Diversification is the Norm:

  • Average rural household has 3-4 income sources
  • Only 20% depend solely on agriculture
  • Mix of crop farming, livestock, wage labor, non-farm activities

Seasonal Patterns:

  • Kharif Season (Jun-Sep): Crop cultivation, farm labor demand high
  • Rabi Season (Oct-Mar): Different crops, irrigation dependent
  • Lean Season (Apr-May): Limited agricultural work, migration peaks

Key Constraints:

  • Small and fragmented landholdings (average: 1.08 hectares)
  • Dependence on monsoon and climate variability
  • Limited access to technology, credit, and markets
  • Weak rural infrastructure and connectivity

Urban Livelihood Strategies

Rural Livelihoods

  • Primary: Agriculture, livestock, fisheries
  • Secondary: Agro-processing, handicrafts, construction
  • Services: Trade, transport, domestic work
  • Characteristics: Seasonal, weather-dependent, low cash income
  • Assets: Land, traditional skills, family labor
  • Challenges: Market access, technology, climate risks

Urban Livelihoods

  • Formal: Manufacturing, IT, services, government jobs
  • Informal: Street vending, domestic work, construction
  • Self-employed: Small shops, services, home-based work
  • Characteristics: Daily wage, high competition, irregular
  • Assets: Skills, networks, location advantages
  • Challenges: Housing, health, social security

The Informal Economy

📊 India's Informal Economy Reality

Scale: 90% of India's workforce is in the informal economy

Characteristics:

  • No formal contracts: Verbal agreements, daily negotiations
  • Irregular income: Daily/weekly earnings, seasonal variations
  • Limited social protection: No insurance, pension, or job security
  • Workplace vulnerability: Poor working conditions, safety risks
  • Multiple activities: Portfolio approach to income generation

Examples by Sector:

Sector Rural Examples Urban Examples
Manufacturing Handloom weaving, pottery, food processing Garment workers, shoe making, electronics assembly
Services Transport, small retail, hair cutting Domestic work, security guards, street vendors
Construction House building, farm structures Building construction, road work, painting
Trade Weekly markets, door-to-door sales Street vendors, wholesale trade helpers

Migration and Livelihoods

Internal Migration in India: 139 million interstate migrants (2011 Census), with millions more seasonal/circular migrants not captured in official data

Migration Type Duration Common Routes Livelihood Activities Challenges
Seasonal 3-6 months Rural → urban/industrial areas Construction, brick kilns, sugarcane cutting Family separation, poor working conditions
Circular Regular short trips Villages → nearby towns Daily wage labor, market trading Transport costs, irregular work
Permanent Long-term relocation Distressed areas → growing cities Informal services, manufacturing Housing, documentation, social integration

Part 3: Vulnerability, Risks, and Coping Strategies

15 minutes

Understanding Livelihood Vulnerability

⚠️ Vulnerability = Risk + Inability to Cope

Risk: Probability of adverse events that threaten livelihoods

Coping Capacity: Ability to manage, resist, or recover from risks

Types of Risks:

  • Covariate: Affect entire communities (drought, floods, economic shocks)
  • Idiosyncratic: Affect individual households (illness, job loss, death)
  • Predictable: Known patterns (seasonal unemployment, aging)
  • Unpredictable: Sudden shocks (natural disasters, health emergencies)

Common Livelihood Risks in India

Risk Category Specific Risks Who's Most Affected Typical Impacts
Climate/Environmental Drought, floods, crop pests, soil degradation Farmers, agricultural laborers Crop loss, food insecurity, migration
Economic Market price volatility, unemployment, inflation Informal workers, small producers Income loss, debt, asset sales

Coping and Adaptation Strategies

🛡️ How People Manage Livelihood Risks

Risk Management Strategies:

  • Prevention: Diversification, skill development, asset building
  • Mitigation: Insurance, savings, technology adoption
  • Coping: Borrowing, asset sales, labor adjustments
  • Recovery: Rebuilding assets, learning from experience

Common Coping Mechanisms in India:

  • Economic: Multiple income sources, seasonal migration, credit
  • Social: Family support, community networks, reciprocity
  • Asset-based: Livestock sales, land mortgaging, gold pawning
  • Consumption: Dietary changes, postponing expenses, child labor
  • Mobility: Temporary migration, changing occupations

Resilience vs. Survival

Survival Strategies

  • Focus: Immediate crisis management
  • Time horizon: Short-term relief
  • Examples: Borrowing at high interest, selling productive assets, child labor
  • Outcome: May worsen long-term situation
  • Emotion: Desperation, limited choices

Resilience Strategies

  • Focus: Building adaptive capacity
  • Time horizon: Long-term strengthening
  • Examples: Skill development, asset accumulation, network building
  • Outcome: Better prepared for future shocks
  • Emotion: Hope, agency, planning

Part 4: Livelihood Development Approaches and Programs

15 minutes

Livelihood Intervention Approaches

🎯 Common Livelihood Development Strategies

1. Asset Building Approach

  • Focus: Strengthen the five capitals (human, natural, financial, physical, social)
  • Examples: Skills training, microcredit, infrastructure development
  • Strength: Addresses root causes, builds long-term capacity
  • Challenge: Takes time, requires sustained investment

2. Value Chain Development

  • Focus: Improve market linkages and value addition
  • Examples: Producer groups, quality improvement, direct marketing
  • Strength: Market-oriented, sustainable income growth
  • Challenge: Requires market knowledge, coordination

3. Social Protection

  • Focus: Reduce vulnerability through safety nets
  • Examples: Employment guarantee, insurance, cash transfers
  • Strength: Immediate relief, predictable support
  • Challenge: May create dependency, targeting challenges

4. Ecosystem Development

  • Focus: Create enabling environment for livelihood growth
  • Examples: Business development services, financial inclusion, policy advocacy
  • Strength: Systemic change, benefits many
  • Challenge: Complex, requires multi-stakeholder coordination

Design Principles for Effective Livelihood Programs

🎨 Key Design Considerations

1. Context Specificity

  • Understand local livelihood systems and constraints
  • Build on existing assets and strategies
  • Consider cultural and social factors

2. Participation and Ownership

  • Involve communities in planning and implementation
  • Build on existing social institutions
  • Ensure representation of marginalized groups

3. Integrated Approach

  • Address multiple constraints simultaneously
  • Link different types of interventions
  • Coordinate across sectors and levels

4. Market Orientation

  • Understand demand and market dynamics
  • Build sustainable business models
  • Focus on quality and competitiveness

5. Sustainability and Scale

  • Build local capacity and institutions
  • Create financial sustainability mechanisms
  • Design for replication and scaling

💡 Program Design Challenge (10 minutes)

Your Mission: Design a livelihood intervention for post-COVID recovery

Target Group: Urban migrant workers who returned to villages during COVID

Profile: 25-45 years old, some skills, limited rural livelihood options, reluctant to migrate again

Assets: Urban work experience, some savings, village connections, basic education

Constraints: Limited local opportunities, skill-employment mismatch, financial stress

Design Framework (Work individually, then pair):

  1. Theory of Change (3 min): If we do X, then Y will happen, leading to Z outcome
  2. Intervention Mix (3 min): Which combination of approaches would you use?
    • □ Skills development □ Financial services □ Market linkages
    • □ Asset building □ Technology □ Social protection
  3. Implementation Strategy (2 min): How would you sequence and deliver interventions?
  4. Success Indicators (2 min): How would you measure impact?

Pair Discussion: Share your design and get feedback on one key assumption

Key Takeaway

Effective livelihood development goes beyond creating jobs or increasing income - it's about building people's capabilities and assets so they can pursue diverse, sustainable, and dignified economic opportunities that fit their context and aspirations.
