Political Economy 101

Workshop 1: Power, Incentives & Institutions in Policy-Making

75-90 Minutes

Understanding the Forces Behind Policy Outcomes

Target Audience: Development practitioners, policy analysts, researchers, and anyone seeking to understand why policies succeed or fail

Prerequisites: Basic understanding of policy processes; familiarity with Indian governance helpful but not required

Materials Needed: Recent policy documents, newspapers, case study materials

Learning Objectives

By the end of this workshop, participants will be able to:

  • Apply political economy frameworks to analyze policy outcomes in Indian contexts
  • Identify how different actors' incentives shape policy design and implementation
  • Distinguish between formal and informal institutions and their policy impacts
  • Analyze power dynamics and coalition politics in specific policy areas
  • Use stakeholder mapping and incentive analysis in their own work

Part 1: Beyond Technical Solutions - Why Politics Matters

20 minutes

üè• Opening Puzzle: Two States, Same Health Policy, Different Outcomes

The Policy: National Rural Health Mission (NRHM) launched in 2005 with identical funding and guidelines across states

Kerala Results (2005-2015):

  • Infant mortality dropped from 12 to 7 per 1,000 births
  • 95% institutional deliveries achieved
  • Strong community participation in health committees

Bihar Results (2005-2015):

  • Infant mortality dropped from 62 to 42 per 1,000 births (still 6x Kerala)
  • 65% institutional deliveries
  • Widespread corruption scandals in health procurement

The Question: Same policy, same money, same timeframe. What explains the dramatic difference in outcomes?

üîë The Political Economy Lens

Political Economy examines how political and economic forces interact to shape policy outcomes. It asks not just "what should be done?" but "what will actually happen and why?"

Three Core Elements:

  • Actors: Who has power and influence? (politicians, bureaucrats, interest groups, citizens)
  • Incentives: What motivates different actors? (electoral, financial, bureaucratic, ideological)
  • Institutions: What are the formal and informal "rules of the game"?

üí° Quick Reflection (5 minutes)

Think of a policy or program you've worked on that didn't achieve expected results. Write down:

  1. Who were the key actors involved?
  2. What were their main incentives or motivations?
  3. What formal or informal rules shaped their behavior?

We'll return to these examples throughout the workshop.

Part 2: Mapping Actors and Incentives

25 minutes

Understanding Stakeholder Incentives

Actor Type Primary Incentives Power Sources Typical Constraints
Politicians Re-election, party advancement, legacy Legislative authority, party networks, media access Electoral cycles, coalition politics, resource limits
Bureaucrats Career advancement, avoiding blame, professional reputation Information, implementation control, technical expertise Rules and procedures, political interference, resource constraints
Interest Groups Policy benefits for members, organizational survival Resources, expertise, mobilization capacity Collective action problems, competition with other groups
Citizens/Voters Public services, economic benefits, representation Voting, protests, civil society engagement Information gaps, collective action challenges

üìä Case Example: MGNREGA Implementation Variations

Andhra Pradesh Success Story:

  • Political Leadership: Chief Minister made rural employment a flagship program
  • Bureaucratic Innovation: Created dedicated implementation teams with performance incentives
  • Civil Society: Strong social audit traditions ensured transparency
  • Alignment of Incentives: Politicians gained from visible employment, bureaucrats rewarded for performance, citizens organized for accountability

Contrast with States with Poor Implementation:

  • Politicians prioritized other programs with higher visibility
  • Bureaucrats lacked clear performance metrics
  • Weak civil society oversight enabled rent-seeking
  • Misaligned Incentives: Personal benefits vs. program objectives

üéØ Stakeholder Mapping Exercise (15 minutes)

Small Groups (3-4 people):

Scenario: A state government wants to implement a new digital literacy program for rural women.

Step 1 (5 min): Identify all relevant stakeholders

Step 2 (5 min): For each stakeholder, map:

  • What they want (incentives)
  • What power they have
  • How they might help or hinder the program

Step 3 (5 min): Identify potential conflicts and areas for alignment

Debrief: Groups share one key insight about incentive alignment or conflict

Part 3: Institutions - The Rules of the Game

20 minutes
üèõÔ∏è Formal Institutions

Definition: Written rules, laws, constitutions, official procedures

Examples:

  • Constitutional provisions
  • Parliamentary procedures
  • Bureaucratic rules
  • Electoral laws

Characteristics: Explicit, legally enforceable, slower to change

ü§ù Informal Institutions

Definition: Unwritten norms, traditions, social practices, networks

Examples:

  • Caste-based social hierarchies
  • Patronage networks
  • Regional political cultures
  • Bureaucratic norms

Characteristics: Implicit, socially enforced, can change quickly or persist for centuries

‚öñÔ∏è When Formal and Informal Institutions Clash

The 73rd Amendment (1992): Mandated 33% reservation for women in panchayats

Formal Rule: Women must hold leadership positions

Informal Practice in Many Areas: Male relatives make decisions; women leaders remain figureheads

Result: Formal compliance but limited substantive change in many contexts (though significant change in others where local norms were more supportive)

üèõÔ∏è Institution Analysis: Kerala vs. Bihar Health Outcomes Revisited

Kerala's Institutional Advantages:

  • Formal: Strong public health infrastructure dating to pre-independence
  • Informal: Social consensus on health as public good; high social mobility expectations
  • Political: Competitive politics with strong civil society oversight
  • Bureaucratic: Merit-based recruitment; professional medical cadre

Bihar's Institutional Challenges:

  • Formal: Weak administrative systems; irregular transfers
  • Informal: Hierarchical social structures; patronage-based networks
  • Political: Politics focused on identity rather than service delivery
  • Bureaucratic: Frequent transfers; political interference in postings

Part 4: Practical Application Framework

15 minutes

üîç The PDIA Framework for Policy Analysis

P - Problem: What is the real problem? (not just symptoms)

D - Drivers: What political and economic forces drive the problem?

I - Interventions: What solutions are politically feasible?

A - Adaptation: How can interventions be adapted based on political feedback?

üõ†Ô∏è Framework Application (10 minutes)

Individual Work: Apply the PDIA framework to a current policy challenge you're familiar with

  1. Problem: Define the core issue (3 sentences max)
  2. Drivers: List 3-4 key political economy factors driving the problem
  3. Interventions: Identify 2-3 politically feasible solutions
  4. Adaptation: How would you test and adjust your approach?

Pair Share: Discuss with a partner - focus on the "drivers" analysis

Key Takeaway

Political economy analysis isn't about being cynical - it's about being realistic. Understanding power, incentives, and institutions helps us design better policies and navigate implementation challenges more effectively.
