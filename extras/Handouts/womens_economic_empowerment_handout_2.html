Problem Set 3: Real Program Analysis

Intermediate
üåü Success Story: Jeevika (Bihar Rural Livelihoods Project)

Program overview: World Bank-supported program reaching 1 million women across Bihar through self-help groups and rural livelihoods interventions.

Key Results (2007-2018):

  • Participation: 1,00,000 SHGs formed, 1 million women members
  • Financial inclusion: ‚Çπ2,500 crores credit mobilized, 95% repayment rate
  • Income impact: 45% increase in average household income
  • Empowerment gains: 60% women report increased decision-making power
  • Social outcomes: 40% increase in girl child enrollment, 25% reduction in domestic violence

Problem 3.2: Scaling and Sustainability Analysis

ü§î Critical Analysis Questions
  1. Scalability factors: What elements of Jeevika's success can be replicated in other states? What are state-specific factors?
  2. Sustainability concerns: How dependent is the program on continued external funding? What happens when World Bank support ends?
  3. Empowerment vs. welfare: Are the outcomes primarily welfare improvements or genuine empowerment? How can you tell the difference?
  4. Institutional change: Has the program changed government systems and approaches to rural development? What evidence would you look for?
  5. Unintended consequences: Are there any negative effects or exclusions? Who might be left behind by this approach?

üìè Key Design Principles from Global Evidence
  1. Integrate multiple interventions: Address constraints simultaneously rather than sequentially
  2. Start with poorest women: Graduation approaches work better than general programs for extreme poor
  3. Leverage technology thoughtfully: Digital tools can reduce costs and increase access, but require supporting ecosystems
  4. Build on existing social structures: Work with, not against, cultural norms and community institutions
  5. Invest in implementation systems: Strong delivery systems matter more than perfect program design
  6. Plan for scale from beginning: Pilot approaches that can work at million-person scale
  7. Measure empowerment, not just income: Track agency, decision-making, and social status changes

üéØ Workshop Synthesis

Effective WEE programming requires evidence-based design, rigorous evaluation, and continuous adaptation. Success comes from addressing multiple constraints simultaneously, measuring multidimensional empowerment outcomes, and building programs that work within existing institutional contexts while gradually transforming them.
