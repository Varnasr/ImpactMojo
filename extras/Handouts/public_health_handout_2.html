Part 1: Social Determinants and Health Equity

25 minutes

Addressing Root Causes of Health Inequalities

Health Equity Principle: Everyone should have a fair opportunity to attain their highest level of health. Health inequities are systematic differences in health outcomes that are socially produced, unfair, and avoidable through reasonable policy and program interventions.

‚öñÔ∏è Case Study: Maternal Mortality Reduction in Tamil Nadu

Challenge: High maternal mortality rates, especially among poor and rural women

Social Determinants Analysis:

  • Economic: Poverty limiting access to care, women lacking financial autonomy
  • Educational: Low literacy affecting health-seeking behavior and care compliance
  • Social: Early marriage, gender discrimination, caste-based exclusion
  • Geographic: Rural areas with poor transportation and limited facilities
  • Health System: Provider shortages, quality gaps, cultural insensitivity

Multi-Level Intervention Strategy:

  • Individual Level: Reproductive health education, birth preparedness counseling
  • Community Level: Self-help groups, traditional birth attendant training, emergency transport
  • Health System Level: Skill development, quality improvement, emergency obstetric care
  • Policy Level: Free delivery care, financial incentives, reservation for marginalized groups

Equity-Focused Results:

  • Maternal Mortality Ratio reduced from 130 to 60 per 100,000 live births
  • Rural-urban gap narrowed significantly through targeted interventions
  • Institutional delivery rates increased from 57% to 99%
  • Reductions greatest among scheduled castes and tribes

Part 2: Health Systems Strengthening

20 minutes

Building Resilient and Responsive Health Systems

Systems Thinking: Health systems are complex adaptive systems where interventions in one area affect multiple other components. Effective strengthening requires understanding these interconnections and designing integrated approaches.

üèóÔ∏è Health System Building Blocks

Building Block Key Components Common Challenges in South Asia Strengthening Strategies
Service Delivery Comprehensive, integrated, quality services Fragmented care, poor quality, urban bias Care coordination, quality improvement, service integration
Health Workforce Adequate numbers, skills, motivation, distribution Shortages, skill mismatches, poor retention Training programs, incentive systems, task-shifting
Health Information Production, analysis, dissemination, use Poor data quality, limited use, fragmentation Digital health systems, capacity building, data governance
Medical Technologies Medicines, vaccines, devices, procedures Access barriers, quality issues, inappropriate use Supply chain management, regulation, rational use
Financing Revenue generation, pooling, purchasing Low public spending, high out-of-pocket costs Universal health coverage, strategic purchasing
Leadership & Governance Oversight, regulation, accountability Weak governance, limited accountability Institutional strengthening, transparency, participation

üöÄ Digital Health Innovation: Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission

Systems Integration Approach: Building unified digital health infrastructure

System Components:

  • Health ID: Unique identifier for every citizen linking health records
  • Healthcare Providers Registry: Database of qualified healthcare professionals
  • Health Facility Registry: Repository of all health facilities and services
  • Electronic Health Records: Interoperable digital health records

Systems Strengthening Impact:

  • Service Delivery: Continuity of care, reduced duplication, better coordination
  • Information Systems: Real-time data, evidence-based decisions, performance monitoring
  • Governance: Transparency, accountability, quality assurance
  • Financing: Claims processing, fraud reduction, outcome-based payments

Implementation Challenges:

  • Digital divide and technology access barriers
  • Privacy and data security concerns
  • Resistance to change from healthcare providers
  • Interoperability across different systems and states
Health System Performance Indicators:
Access = (Population with essential services / Total population) √ó 100
Quality = (Services meeting quality standards / Total services) √ó 100
Efficiency = Health outcomes achieved / Resources invested
Equity = Difference in health outcomes between best and worst performing groups
Resilience = System capacity to maintain functions during shocks

üèòÔ∏è Case Study: Community-Led Total Sanitation (CLTS) in Bangladesh

Approach: Community-driven behavior change for open defecation elimination

Community Engagement Strategy:

  • Triggering: Community mapping of defecation areas, "walk of shame" to highlight problems
  • Social Mobilization: Peer pressure, collective commitment to become open defecation free
  • Local Innovation: Community designs own sanitation solutions using local materials
  • Collective Action: Community monitoring, celebration of achievements

Health System Support:

  • Facilitation: Trained facilitators trigger community processes
  • Technical Support: Provide sanitation options and technical guidance
  • Monitoring: Track progress and celebrate achievements
  • Scaling: Document and replicate successful approaches

Community Empowerment Outcomes:

  • 98% reduction in open defecation in program areas
  • 50% reduction in diarrheal diseases among children
  • Increased community confidence to address other health issues
  • Model replicated in 60+ countries globally

üéØ Community Health Worker Integration

ASHA (Accredited Social Health Activist) Model in India:

  • Community Selection: Women chosen by and accountable to communities
  • Comprehensive Training: Health promotion, basic treatment, referral systems
  • Performance Incentives: Task-based payments linked to health outcomes
  • System Integration: Formal links to health facilities and programs
  • Continuous Support: Ongoing training, supervision, resource provision

Community-System Bridge Functions:

  • Health education and behavior change communication
  • Basic diagnostic and treatment services
  • Referral and escort services to health facilities
  • Community mobilization for health programs
  • Data collection and reporting on community health

Part 4: Public Health Advocacy and Policy

15 minutes

From Evidence to Policy Impact

üì¢ Public Health Advocacy Strategies

  • Evidence Generation: Research, surveillance data, community documentation
  • Coalition Building: Multi-stakeholder alliances, professional networks, civil society
  • Media Engagement: Strategic communication, story-telling, crisis response
  • Policy Analysis: Cost-benefit studies, implementation feasibility, political mapping
  • Direct Advocacy: Legislative testimony, policy briefings, stakeholder meetings

üö≠ Advocacy Success: Tobacco Control in India

Multi-Pronged Advocacy Strategy:

  • Evidence Base: Research on tobacco health impacts, economic costs
  • Coalition: Medical associations, NGOs, international partners
  • Media Campaign: Public awareness, highlighting health consequences
  • Policy Advocacy: Parliamentary committee testimony, policy recommendations
  • Legal Action: Public interest litigation, enforcement support

Policy Outcomes:

  • Cigarettes and Other Tobacco Products Act (COTPA) 2003
  • Graphic health warnings on tobacco products
  • Ban on tobacco advertising and sponsorship
  • Smoke-free public places legislation
  • Higher taxation on tobacco products
