Part 1: From Measurement to Learning - Shifting the MEL Mindset

20 minutes
üìä Traditional M&E

Purpose: Prove success to donors

Focus: Compliance and reporting

Timing: End-of-project evaluation

Ownership: M&E specialist/external evaluator

Questions: "Did we achieve our targets?"

Response to Failure: Justify or hide problems

üîÑ Adaptive MEL

Purpose: Improve programs through learning

Focus: Understanding what works, for whom, why

Timing: Continuous learning cycles

Ownership: Entire program team + stakeholders

Questions: "How can we do better?"

Response to Failure: Analyze and adapt quickly

üìö Case Study: Pratham's Teaching at the Right Level (TaRL)

The Challenge: Millions of Indian children in schools but not learning basic reading and math

Traditional Approach Would Have Been: Design curriculum, train teachers, measure test scores at end

Pratham's Adaptive Approach:

  • Rapid Learning Cycles: 10-day camps with immediate assessment
  • Real-time Feedback: Daily reflection with teachers and children
  • Continuous Adaptation: Modified methods based on what worked locally
  • Stakeholder Involvement: Parents, children, teachers co-designed improvements
  • Rigorous Testing: Randomized controlled trials to validate learning

Result: Scaled from 1 state to 19 states and multiple countries, with continuous innovation

üîÑ The Adaptive Learning Cycle

PLAN ‚Üí IMPLEMENT ‚Üí LEARN ‚Üí ADAPT ‚Üí PLAN

Short cycles (weeks/months) with rapid feedback and course correction

üí≠ MEL Mindset Check (8 minutes)

Individual Reflection (5 min): Think about the MEL system in your current/recent program

Rate your program (1-5 scale):

  • How quickly do you get feedback on what's working? (1=annually, 5=weekly)
  • How much do beneficiaries shape evaluation questions? (1=not at all, 5=they lead)
  • How often do findings lead to program changes? (1=never, 5=regularly)
  • How comfortable is your team with discussing failures? (1=very uncomfortable, 5=openly encouraged)

Pair Share (3 min): What's one thing that could shift your MEL toward more learning?

Part 2: Designing Adaptive MEL Systems

25 minutes

Key Components of Learning-Oriented MEL

üéØ 1. Learning Questions (Not Just Indicators)

Traditional: "How many people were trained?"

Adaptive: "What training approaches work best for different types of participants?"

Key Learning Questions to Ask:

  • What's working well and why?
  • What's not working and why?
  • What assumptions are proving wrong?
  • What unexpected results are emerging?
  • How is context shaping outcomes?

‚ö° 2. Rapid Feedback Loops

Design for Speed: Monthly reflection meetings, quarterly deep dives, annual strategic reviews

Multi-Channel Feedback:

  • Formal: Surveys, focus groups, key informant interviews
  • Informal: Staff observations, beneficiary complaints, partner feedback
  • Digital: SMS feedback, social media monitoring, app analytics
  • Participatory: Community scorecards, social audits, peer evaluation

üîÑ 3. Structured Learning Processes

After Action Reviews (AAR): What was supposed to happen? What actually happened? Why were there differences? What can we learn?

Learning Sessions: Regular team meetings focused on:

  • Sharing challenges and solutions across team members
  • Analyzing data together to identify patterns
  • Brainstorming adaptations based on findings
  • Planning tests of new approaches

üåü Building a Learning Culture

Safe to Fail: Celebrate learning from mistakes; reward honest reporting of problems

Curiosity Over Certainty: Ask "what if?" and "why?" more than "did we succeed?"

Data for Decisions: Use data to make program improvements, not just donor reports

Stakeholder Voice: Include beneficiaries and partners in defining success and learning

üè• Example: Adaptive MEL in Rural Health Programs

Program: Improving maternal health services in Odisha villages

Learning Question: "How can we increase institutional deliveries among tribal women?"

Rapid Learning Approach:

  • Monthly Community Meetings: ASHAs and women discuss barriers and solutions
  • Real-time Data: WhatsApp group for field staff to share challenges immediately
  • Quarterly Adaptation: Program modifications based on emerging patterns
  • Annual Evaluation: Rigorous assessment of what worked where and why

Key Learning & Adaptation:

  • Discovered transport was bigger barrier than awareness
  • Adapted by negotiating with local transport providers
  • Found cultural practices varied significantly by village
  • Shifted to village-specific approaches rather than standard model

üõ†Ô∏è Design Your Learning System (15 minutes)

Small Groups (3-4 people): Use a current or past program

Step 1 (5 min): Identify 2-3 key learning questions for your program

Step 2 (5 min): Design feedback loops: What data would you collect? How often? From whom?

Step 3 (5 min): Plan learning processes: When would your team reflect? How would you share findings? How would you make decisions to adapt?

Guiding Questions:

  • What do you most need to learn to improve your program?
  • Who has the best insights about what's working/not working?
  • How quickly do you need feedback to make useful adjustments?

Part 3: Participatory Evaluation Methods

25 minutes

Why Participatory Evaluation Matters

üë• Core Principles of Participatory Evaluation

Stakeholder Ownership: Those affected by the program shape evaluation questions and methods

Capacity Building: Evaluation process builds local skills and knowledge

Utilization Focus: Designed to be used by stakeholders for decision-making

Democratic Values: Power-sharing in evaluation design and implementation

Cultural Responsiveness: Methods adapted to local context and preferences

üó≥Ô∏è Community Scorecards

What: Community members rate services and identify priorities

When: Service delivery programs

Time: 2-3 hours community meeting

Output: Action plans for improvement

üé≠ Most Significant Change

What: Collect stories of change; stakeholders vote on most significant

When: Complex programs with diverse outcomes

Time: 1-2 hours story collection + analysis

Output: Prioritized change narratives

üéØ Outcome Harvesting

What: Start with outcomes, work backwards to verify and explain

When: Programs with emergent/unpredictable results

Time: 2-3 days facilitated process

Output: Verified outcome statements

üìä Social Audits

What: Public verification of program implementation and expenditure

When: Government programs, transparency initiatives

Time: Half-day community meeting

Output: Public accountability and corrective actions

üó∫Ô∏è Participatory Video

What: Communities create videos documenting their experiences

When: Voice and advocacy-focused programs

Time: 3-5 days training + production

Output: Community-created evaluation evidence

üîç Peer Evaluation

What: Program participants evaluate each other's progress

When: Skills/behavior change programs

Time: 1-2 hours structured peer review

Output: Peer feedback and learning

üé¨ Participatory Evaluation in Action: Women's Empowerment Program

Context: Self-help group program in rural Rajasthan

Challenge: Traditional surveys weren't capturing women's experiences of empowerment

Participatory Approach:

  • Most Significant Change: Women shared stories of personal transformation
  • Participatory Video: Groups created videos showing before/after scenarios
  • Community Scorecards: Rated different aspects of program support
  • Peer Evaluation: Women assessed each other's leadership growth

Key Insights (missed by traditional M&E):

  • Increased mobility and confidence mattered more than income changes
  • Family support was critical factor, not just individual capacity
  • Program success varied significantly by caste and age groups
  • Women wanted more political engagement opportunities

Program Adaptations: Added family counseling components, created age-specific groups, developed civic engagement track

üéØ Method Selection Exercise (12 minutes)

Continue with your program from previous exercises

Step 1 (4 min): Identify your key stakeholder groups and their different perspectives

Step 2 (4 min): Choose 2 participatory methods that would work for your context

Step 3 (4 min): Plan implementation: Who would facilitate? What resources needed? How would findings be used?

Selection Criteria:

  • Which stakeholders have the most insight into your key learning questions?
  • What methods fit your cultural context and stakeholder capacities?
  • How much time and resources do you realistically have?
  • What methods would generate evidence stakeholders would trust and use?

Part 4: Building Learning into Organizational Systems

15 minutes
Organizational Level Learning Practices Success Indicators
Individual Reflection journals, peer mentoring, learning goals in performance reviews Staff report learning from mistakes; curiosity about beneficiary feedback
Team Regular learning meetings, cross-team exchanges, failure parties Teams adapt programs based on evidence; open discussion of challenges
Organizational Learning budgets, knowledge management systems, learning-focused incentives Programs evolve over time; innovations spread across projects
Sector Knowledge sharing networks, learning events, collaborative evaluations Field-wide improvement in practices; reduced duplication of ineffective approaches

üöÄ Making Learning Stick: Implementation Tips

Start Small: Begin with one program and one learning question

Leadership Buy-in: Ensure senior staff model learning behaviors

Capacity Investment: Train staff in facilitation and participatory methods

Technology Support: Use simple tools for data collection and sharing

External Learning: Connect with other learning-oriented organizations

üèóÔ∏è Learning System Blueprint (10 minutes)

Individual Planning: Design next steps for your organization

Quick Action Planning:

  1. Immediate (Next Month): One small learning practice you could start
  2. Short-term (3 Months): One participatory method you could pilot
  3. Medium-term (6 Months): One organizational change that would support learning
  4. Champions: Who in your organization would support this shift?
  5. Resources: What budget/time would you need to request?

Commitment: Share your immediate next step with someone in the room
