Part 1: Advanced Facilitation for Adult Learners

25 minutes

üéØ Scenario: Training Rural Health Workers on Maternal Care

You're designing a 3-day training for 30 ASHA workers from different villages in Rajasthan. They have varying literacy levels, speak different dialects, and some have been working for 15+ years while others are new. How do you create an engaging, effective learning experience?

The Challenge: Traditional lecture-based training has low retention rates and limited behavior change. You need methods that honor their experience while building new skills.

üß† Adult Learning Principles in Practice

Andragogy vs. Pedagogy: Adults learn differently from children

Adult Learning Characteristic Facilitation Implication Practical Technique
Experience-Rich Build on existing knowledge Start with "What do you already know about...?"
Problem-Oriented Focus on real challenges Use case studies from their actual work
Internally Motivated Connect to personal goals "How will this help you serve your community better?"
Respect-Seeking Honor their expertise Position yourself as co-learner, not expert
Application-Focused Immediate relevance Practice skills they'll use tomorrow

Deep Dive: Needs Assessment and Design

üîç Comprehensive Needs Assessment Framework

Performance Gap Analysis
  • Current vs. desired performance
  • Knowledge, skills, attitude gaps
  • Systemic vs. individual issues
  • Training vs. non-training solutions
Learner Profile Development
  • Demographics and background
  • Prior knowledge and experience
  • Learning preferences and barriers
  • Motivation and readiness to learn
Context and Constraints
  • Organizational culture and support
  • Resource availability and limitations
  • Technology access and infrastructure
  • Timeline and logistics
Stakeholder Analysis
  • Direct beneficiaries and learners
  • Implementing organizations
  • Funders and decision-makers
  • Community and government partners

üå± Case Study: Designing Farmer Climate Adaptation Training

Context: NGO wants to train 500 smallholder farmers across 3 states on climate-smart agriculture practices.

Needs Assessment Findings:

  • Performance Gap: Farmers know traditional methods but lack knowledge of drought-resistant crops and water conservation
  • Learner Profile: 60% women, varied literacy levels, strong experiential knowledge, skeptical of "book learning"
  • Context: Monsoon variability increasing, government extension services limited, strong peer networks
  • Constraints: Limited budget, harvest season timing, poor mobile connectivity

Design Question: How would you design this program to maximize adoption and impact?

üéØ Learning Objectives Workshop (8 minutes)

Challenge: Write clear, measurable learning objectives for the farmer training case study.

Use SMART Framework:

  • Specific - Clear and focused
  • Measurable - Observable behaviors
  • Achievable - Realistic given constraints
  • Relevant - Addresses real needs
  • Time-bound - Clear timeline

Your Task: Write 3 learning objectives at different levels:

  1. Knowledge: What should farmers know?
  2. Skills: What should farmers be able to do?
  3. Application: How should farmers change their practice?

Part 3: Assessment and Evaluation Strategies

20 minutes

üìä Multi-Level Assessment Framework

Kirkpatrick Level What It Measures Assessment Methods Timing
Level 1: Reaction Satisfaction, engagement Feedback forms, focus groups, observation During/immediately after
Level 2: Learning Knowledge, skills, attitudes Pre/post tests, practical demonstrations, portfolios Before, during, end of training
Level 3: Behavior Application in real context Observation, self-reports, peer feedback, case studies 3-6 months post-training
Level 4: Results Impact on outcomes Performance indicators, impact evaluation, ROI analysis 6-12 months post-training

Innovative Assessment Approaches

üì± Digital Portfolios

Learners document their learning journey

  • Video reflections and demonstrations
  • Photo documentation of practice
  • Peer feedback and comments
  • Progress tracking over time
üé≠ Performance-Based Assessment

Real-world application of skills

  • Simulated scenarios and role-plays
  • Project-based assessments
  • Community problem-solving
  • Peer observation and feedback
üîÑ Continuous Assessment

Ongoing feedback and improvement

  • Learning analytics from digital platforms
  • Regular check-ins and coaching
  • Micro-assessments and quick polls
  • Self-assessment and reflection tools
üåê Community-Based Assessment

Validation by beneficiaries

  • Community feedback on services
  • Outcome measurement by users
  • Social validation of learning
  • Impact stories and testimonials

üí° Assessment Design Principles

  • Alignment: Assessments match learning objectives and teaching methods
  • Authenticity: Tasks mirror real-world application contexts
  • Fairness: Multiple ways to demonstrate learning, culturally responsive
  • Transparency: Clear criteria and expectations shared with learners
  • Feedback-Rich: Assessment provides actionable information for improvement

üîÑ Blended Learning Design Framework

The 70-20-10 Model for Development Learning:

  • 70% Experiential: On-the-job learning, real projects, field practice
  • 20% Social: Peer learning, mentoring, communities of practice
  • 10% Formal: Structured training, workshops, online courses
üí° Blended Learning Best Practices
  • Start with learning goals, not technology - choose tools that serve objectives
  • Consider the digital divide - ensure equitable access
  • Build digital literacy gradually - don't assume tech skills
  • Create offline alternatives - technology as enhancement, not requirement
  • Focus on interaction - use technology to connect, not isolate

üéØ Sustainability Framework

Five Dimensions of Educational Program Sustainability:

  • Financial: Diversified funding, cost-effective models, fee-for-service options
  • Institutional: Organizational capacity, leadership development, system integration
  • Technical: Local expertise, knowledge management, continuous improvement
  • Political: Government support, policy alignment, stakeholder buy-in
  • Social: Community ownership, cultural fit, ongoing demand
‚úÖ Program Design Quality Checklist
Needs-Based: Program addresses real, documented learning needs
Learner-Centered: Design considers adult learning principles and learner characteristics
Contextually Appropriate: Methods and content fit local culture and constraints
Outcome-Focused: Clear, measurable learning objectives with aligned assessments
Evidence-Based: Incorporates research on effective educational practices
Sustainable: Financial and institutional sustainability planned from start
Scalable: Design allows for adaptation and growth
Evaluation-Ready: Monitoring and evaluation systems built in
