Part 1: Intersectional Data Analysis in Practice

25 minutes

Beyond Single-Category Analysis

üìä Case Study: NFHS-5 Women's Employment Data

The Standard Story: "Women's workforce participation in India increased from 25.8% to 32.5% between 2015-2021"

The Intersectional Questions:

  • How does this vary by caste, class, religion, and region?
  • What types of work are women entering? Paid? Unpaid? Precarious?
  • Which women are being counted, and which remain invisible?
  • What about women with disabilities, transgender women, single mothers?

üîç Intersectionality Analysis Framework

Identity Dimension Data Questions to Ask Common Blind Spots
Caste Are SC/ST/OBC categories disaggregated? How do outcomes vary? Treating "Hindu" as homogeneous; missing inter-caste marriage data
Class Income/asset quintiles analyzed separately? Urban vs rural poverty? Using only income; ignoring wealth, assets, social capital
Religion Religious minority experiences highlighted? Sectarian differences? Conflating religious identity with economic status
Disability Different disability types included? Accessibility barriers measured? Medical model assumptions; missing data on social barriers
Age Life stage variations? Generational differences in experiences? Binary young/old categories; missing adolescent girls

Part 2: Counter-Data and Alternative Narratives

20 minutes

When Official Data Doesn't Tell the Whole Story

Principle: Counter-data challenges dominant narratives by surfacing what official statistics make invisible, using alternative metrics that center lived experience.

üè† Case Study: Housing in Delhi

Official Story: "Delhi has achieved 95% coverage of pucca housing construction"

Counter-Data Questions:

  • Quality: What about ventilation, water access, sewerage, electricity reliability?
  • Location: How far from employment centers? Access to schools, healthcare?
  • Affordability: What percentage of income goes to housing costs?
  • Security: Legal tenure? Protection from eviction? Women's property rights?
  • Community: Social networks disrupted? Cultural/religious spaces accessible?

üìà Counter-Data Strategies

  • Community-Led Data Collection: Participatory mapping, photovoice, community scorecards
  • Alternative Indicators: Time-use surveys, safety audits, social cohesion measures
  • Qualitative Integration: Story-telling with numbers, lived experience data
  • Temporal Reframing: Historical context, intergenerational effects, seasonal variations
  • Relational Data: Power dynamics, social networks, care relationships

üõ†Ô∏è Exercise 2: Creating Counter-Narratives (15 minutes)

Small Groups (3-4 people, 12 minutes):

Your group is assigned one of these "success stories." Create a counter-data narrative:

Option A: "Digital India: 80% mobile phone penetration achieved"

Option B: "Swachh Bharat: 98% household toilet coverage completed"

Option C: "Financial Inclusion: 78% adults have bank accounts"

For your assigned topic, develop:

  1. Alternative questions (3 minutes): What should we be measuring instead?
  2. Missing voices (3 minutes): Whose experiences are invisible in the official data?
  3. New indicators (3 minutes): What metrics would tell a different story?
  4. Data sources (3 minutes): How could communities collect this data themselves?

Report Back (3 minutes): Each group shares one key insight

Part 3: Feminist Data Visualization

20 minutes

Design Choices as Political Choices

üé® Feminist Viz Principles

Every design choice carries values:

  • Color: Does your palette reinforce gender stereotypes? Is it accessible?
  • Scale: What's included in the frame? What's cropped out?
  • Categories: How are groups defined and labeled? Who gets "othered"?
  • Context: Historical baselines? Structural factors? Power relationships?
  • Narrative: Whose story does the viz tell? Who is the implied audience?
üî¥ Problematic Example

Chart: "Women's Education Progress"

Issues:

  • Only shows gender binary
  • Ignores quality of education
  • No breakdown by caste/class
  • Missing dropout reasons
‚úÖ Feminist Alternative

Chart: "Learning Journeys: Barriers & Supports"

Better because:

  • Shows intersectional breakdowns
  • Includes structural barriers
  • Centers quality metrics
  • Highlights community assets
üî¥ Problematic Example

Chart: "Maternal Mortality Rates"

Issues:

  • Medicalizes structural issues
  • Individual-blame framing
  • Missing healthcare access data
  • No community solutions
‚úÖ Feminist Alternative

Chart: "Birth Justice Ecosystem"

Better because:

  • Maps structural determinants
  • Shows community care networks
  • Includes policy/resource factors
  • Centers women's agency

(Bar chart showing: Household work 45%, No jobs available 25%, Family doesn't allow 20%, Other 10%)

Part 4: Power Analysis of Data Systems

15 minutes

Who Benefits from Current Data Practices?

üîç Data Power Analysis Framework

Power Dimension Key Questions South Asian Examples
Who Defines? Who sets research priorities? Defines variables? Designs surveys? NFHS questions designed by international agencies vs. community priorities
Who Collects? Who has access to communities? What power dynamics affect responses? Upper-caste enumerators in Dalit communities; male researchers asking about gender-based violence
Who Owns? Who controls the data? Sets access rules? Profits from insights? Aadhaar data governance; corporate control of platform worker data
Who Interprets? Who gets to say what the data means? Whose analysis is considered legitimate? Academic researchers vs. community knowledge; English-language publications
Who Benefits? Who gains from insights? Gets resources? Builds careers? Makes policy? Urban consultants designing rural programs; international development industry

üõ†Ô∏è Exercise 4: Data System Power Mapping (10 minutes)

Think about a data project you've been involved in (or heard about):

High Power Actors:

  • Who had decision-making power?
  • Who controlled resources?
  • Who set the agenda?

Low Power Actors:

  • Who provided data but had no control?
  • Who was studied but not consulted?
  • Who was excluded entirely?

Reflection Questions (5 minutes):

  • How could power have been shared more equitably?
  • What would community ownership have looked like?
  • How could the benefits have been redistributed?
