Part 1: India's Judicial Structure - Hierarchy and Jurisdiction

20 minutes

üèõÔ∏è Article 124-147: Independent Judiciary

Constitutional Principle: Separation of powers with independent judiciary as guardian of Constitution

Key Features:

  • Independence: Security of tenure, fixed salaries, contempt powers
  • Appointment: President appoints on consultation with Chief Justice and governors
  • Removal: Impeachment only for "proved misbehaviour or incapacity"
  • Unified System: Single integrated hierarchy (unlike federal systems like USA)

Three-Tier Court System

üèõÔ∏è Supreme Court of India

Jurisdiction: Constitutional, appellate, advisory, original

Composition: 1 Chief Justice + 33 Judges (max)

Key Powers:

  • Guardian of Constitution
  • Final court of appeal
  • Judicial review of laws
  • Constitutional interpretation
  • Article 32 (fundamental rights enforcement)
‚öñÔ∏è High Courts (25 across states/UTs)

Jurisdiction: Original, appellate, supervisory, writ

Composition: Chief Justice + other judges (varies by state)

Key Powers:

  • Constitutional jurisdiction within state
  • Article 226 (broader writ powers than SC)
  • Supervision of subordinate courts
  • Administrative control over district courts
  • PIL and environmental matters
üè¢ District & Subordinate Courts

Jurisdiction: Criminal, civil, revenue, family matters

Structure: District Judge ‚Üí Additional District Judge ‚Üí Civil Judge

Key Functions:

  • Trial courts for most cases
  • Family courts for matrimonial issues
  • Fast track courts for speedy disposal
  • Lok Adalats for alternative dispute resolution
  • Revenue courts for land disputes

Specialized Courts and Tribunals

Article 323A & 323B: Constitutional basis for administrative and other tribunals to reduce burden on regular courts

Tribunal Type Jurisdiction Development Relevance Appeal Route
National Green Tribunal (NGT) Environmental protection, forest rights Environmental clearances, pollution cases Supreme Court
Central Administrative Tribunal Civil service matters, employment Government job disputes, service conditions High Court (limited)
National Company Law Tribunal Corporate law, insolvency Business regulation, corporate accountability National Company Law Appellate Tribunal
Armed Forces Tribunal Military service matters Veterans' rights, military welfare Supreme Court

Part 2: Landmark Constitutional Cases - Development Applications

25 minutes

Foundational Doctrines for Development Work

‚öñÔ∏è Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)

Issue: Can Parliament amend any part of Constitution including fundamental rights?

Supreme Court Ruling: "Basic Structure" doctrine - Parliament cannot destroy essential features of Constitution

Basic Structure Elements:

  • Supremacy of Constitution
  • Democracy and free elections
  • Secularism
  • Federal structure
  • Independence of judiciary
  • Rule of law

Development Implication: Constitutional rights cannot be completely abolished - provides security for rights-based development approaches

‚öñÔ∏è Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)

Issue: Government impounded passport without hearing - violation of right to travel abroad

Revolutionary Ruling: Expanded Article 21 to include due process and dignified procedure

Key Principles:

  • Fundamental rights are interconnected, not isolated
  • Procedure must be just, fair, and reasonable (not just established by law)
  • Right to life includes right to livelihood, travel, privacy

Development Impact: Legal foundation for challenging arbitrary government action affecting livelihoods and dignified life

Social Justice and Development Cases

Case (Year) Constitutional Issue Court Ruling Development Application
Olga Tellis v. BMC (1985) Right to livelihood vs slum removal Right to livelihood part of Article 21 Legal protection for informal sector workers
M.C. Mehta v. Union (1987) Environmental protection as fundamental right Right to healthy environment under Article 21 Environmental clearances, pollution control
Unnikrishnan v. Andhra Pradesh (1993) Right to education scope and limits Free education up to 14 years fundamental right Legal basis for RTE Act, education programs
Indra Sawhney v. Union (1992) Reservation policy constitutional validity 50% ceiling, creamy layer exclusion Affirmative action in development programs
Vishaka v. Rajasthan (1997) Sexual harassment at workplace Guidelines for prevention and redressal Workplace safety, women's economic participation
NALSA v. Union (2014) Transgender rights recognition Third gender recognition, equal rights Inclusive development, identity documentation

üìä Deep Dive: PUCL v. Union of India (Right to Food Cases)

Background: Series of cases starting 2001 during Rajasthan drought

Constitutional Foundation: Article 21 (right to life) includes right to food with dignity

Major Court Orders & Impact:

2001 - Immediate Relief

Emergency food distribution in drought-affected areas, employment programs

2004 - Midday Meal Scheme

Cooked meals mandatory in all government primary schools

2005 - MGNREGA Framework

Employment guarantee as legal entitlement, not welfare

2013 - Food Security Act

Legislative framework for food security as legal right

Advocacy Lessons:

  • Strategic litigation can drive policy change
  • Constitutional rights provide powerful legal tools
  • Court monitoring ensures implementation
  • Legal advocacy requires sustained engagement

Part 3: Constitutional Remedies - Writs and Public Interest Litigation

20 minutes

üõ°Ô∏è Article 32: Right to Constitutional Remedies

"Heart and Soul of Constitution" - Dr. B.R. Ambedkar

Constitutional Guarantee: Right to directly approach Supreme Court for enforcement of fundamental rights

Powers: Supreme Court can issue writs including habeas corpus, mandamus, prohibition, certiorari, quo-warranto

Five Constitutional Writs - Legal Tools for Development Advocacy

üìú Habeas Corpus ("Have the Body")

Purpose: Protect against illegal detention

When to Use: Arbitrary arrest, disappearances, illegal custody

Development Context: Protecting activists, ensuring due process in development conflicts

Example: Environmental activists detained without proper charges

üìã Mandamus ("We Command")

Purpose: Compel public officials to perform legal duties

When to Use: Government inaction, service delivery failures

Development Context: Force implementation of welfare schemes, infrastructure projects

Example: Compelling government to provide clean drinking water

üö´ Prohibition ("Forbid")

Purpose: Prevent lower courts/tribunals from exceeding jurisdiction

When to Use: Jurisdictional overreach, ultra vires actions

Development Context: Challenging inappropriate forum selection

Example: Preventing revenue court from deciding constitutional matters

üîç Certiorari ("To be Certified")

Purpose: Quash orders of lower courts/tribunals

When to Use: Jurisdictional errors, procedural violations

Development Context: Challenging flawed administrative decisions

Example: Quashing environmental clearance granted without proper assessment

‚ùì Quo-Warranto ("By What Authority")

Purpose: Challenge illegal appointments to public office

When to Use: Unqualified appointments, procedural violations

Development Context: Ensuring qualified governance in development institutions

Example: Challenging appointment of unqualified person to environment board

Public Interest Litigation (PIL) - Democratizing Justice

üåü PIL Revolution: Justice for the Marginalized

Innovation: Relaxed standing rules - anyone can approach court for public interest

Foundation: S.P. Gupta case (1981) - "Easy access to justice for poor and marginalized"

Procedure: Even postcard to Supreme Court can initiate PIL

PIL Success Stories in Development:

  • Bandhua Mukti Morcha: Bonded labor liberation
  • M.C. Mehta Environmental Cases: Pollution control, Taj Mahal protection
  • Right to Food Cases: Food security legislation
  • Right to Education: Infrastructure and quality standards

üìã PIL Template for Development Issues

1. Identify Constitutional Violation

  • Which fundamental right is violated?
  • How does it affect public interest?
  • Who are the affected groups?

2. Establish Locus Standi

  • Why are you filing (personal interest not required)
  • Demonstrate genuine public interest concern
  • Show affected parties cannot approach court themselves

3. Document Evidence

  • Government orders, policies, circulars
  • Survey data, research studies
  • Media reports, ground testimonies
  • Comparative analysis from other states/countries

4. Frame Relief Sought

  • Specific, actionable directions
  • Timeline for implementation
  • Monitoring mechanism
  • Interim relief for urgent matters

Issue: Contaminated water supply in urban slums affecting 50,000 residents

Situation: Municipal corporation supplies untreated water to slum areas. High incidence of waterborne diseases. Children missing school due to illness. No response to complaints for 2 years.

PIL Drafting Exercise (6 minutes):

  1. Constitutional Rights: Which fundamental rights are violated?
  2. Legal Precedents: Which cases support your argument?
  3. Relief Sought: What specific orders do you want from court?
  4. Writ Type: Which writ is most appropriate?

Peer Review (2 minutes): Exchange drafts and provide feedback on legal strategy

Part 4: Constitutional Evolution - Amendments and Adaptation

10 minutes

üìù Article 368: Amendment Process

Three Types of Amendment Procedures:

  • Simple Majority: Some provisions (admission of new states)
  • Special Majority: Most provisions (2/3 majority of present + voting + majority of total membership)
  • Special Majority + State Ratification: Federal structure provisions (Article 368, judicial powers, distribution of powers)

Total Amendments: 105 amendments since 1950 (average: 1.4 per year)

Transformative Constitutional Amendments for Development

Amendment Year Key Changes Development Impact
25th Amendment 1971 Right to property not absolute, compensation determination Land reforms, public sector expansion
42nd Amendment 1976 Added Socialist, Secular to Preamble; Fundamental Duties State-led development, secular governance
44th Amendment 1978 Right to property removed from fundamental rights Easier land acquisition for development projects
73rd Amendment 1992 Constitutional status to Panchayati Raj Grassroots democracy, rural development
74th Amendment 1992 Constitutional status to urban local bodies Urban governance, municipal services
86th Amendment 2002 Right to education (6-14 years) as fundamental right Universal primary education, RTE Act
101st Amendment 2016 Goods and Services Tax (GST) Unified tax system, economic integration

Judicial Review vs Parliamentary Sovereignty: Indian system balances both - Parliament can amend Constitution but cannot destroy its basic structure (Kesavananda Bharati doctrine)

Practical Exercise: Legal Advocacy Strategy

10 minutes

üéØ Complete Legal Strategy Design

Complex Scenario: Mining company operations violating tribal land rights and environmental laws

Case Facts: Mining company operating in tribal area without proper consent. Environmental clearance obtained through fraudulent documents. Forest land diverted illegally. Tribal communities displaced, water sources polluted. State government supporting company.

Comprehensive Strategy (8 minutes):

  1. Constitutional Analysis: Which rights violated? Relevant constitutional provisions?
  2. Legal Precedents: Supportive case law for tribal rights, environment protection?
  3. Forum Selection: High Court or Supreme Court? Why?
  4. Relief Strategy: Interim relief + final relief structure?
  5. Evidence Plan: What documentation needed to prove violations?
  6. Implementation Monitoring: How to ensure court orders are followed?

Strategy Presentation (2 minutes): One group presents integrated legal advocacy approach

Key Takeaway

India's constitutional framework provides powerful tools for development advocacy through an independent judiciary, fundamental rights enforcement, and public interest litigation. Understanding court structures, landmark precedents, and legal remedies enables practitioners to effectively advance social justice through law.
