Part 1: Systematic Qualitative Data Analysis

25 minutes

From Raw Data to Meaningful Patterns

üìù The Qualitative Analysis Process

Analysis is not a separate stage‚Äîit begins during data collection and continues through writing. The goal is to move from descriptive accounts to analytical insights that answer your research questions.

üîÑ Iterative Analysis Framework

Stage Activities Output Quality Checks
1. Data Familiarization Read transcripts, listen to recordings, write memos Initial impressions, emerging themes Multiple readings, reflexive notes
2. Initial Coding Open coding, line-by-line analysis Preliminary codes, coded segments Consistent application, code definitions
3. Focused Coding Refine codes, identify patterns Code families, conceptual categories Inter-coder reliability, code saturation
4. Thematic Development Build themes, test relationships Thematic map, theoretical model Negative case analysis, peer review
5. Interpretation Connect to theory, contextualize Analytical narrative, implications Member checking, triangulation
üìå Descriptive Codes

What is happening?

Direct labeling of content: "barriers to healthcare," "family support," "economic hardship"

Example: "Women often delay seeking treatment because of transportation costs"

üîç Interpretive Codes

What does this mean?

Analytical interpretation: "prioritizing family over self," "structural barriers," "agency vs. constraint"

Example: This reflects gendered expectations about women's roles and self-sacrifice

üìã In-Vivo Codes

Participants' own words

Preserve exact language: "double burden," "fighting the system," "making do"

Example: "We are always adjusting"‚Äîcaptures adaptive strategies

üîó Pattern Codes

Relationships between codes

Meta-codes connecting themes: "systemic exclusion," "informal networks," "resistance strategies"

Example: How informal support systems compensate for state failures

üõ†Ô∏è Exercise 1: Hands-On Coding Practice (18 minutes)

Individual Work (12 minutes):

Sample Interview Excerpt:

"After my husband died, I had to start working as a domestic help. The children were small, and there was no other option. My in-laws said I should manage somehow, but they didn't help. The work is hard, and the madam sometimes doesn't pay on time. But what can I do? I have to feed my children. My eldest daughter now helps me with the younger ones after school. Sometimes I worry she's missing her childhood, but this is our reality. The government schemes... I tried to get the widow pension, but the official asked for so many papers, and then wanted money under the table. I couldn't afford it. So we manage with what we have. My neighbors help sometimes, especially Kamala aunty. She watches the children when I have to work late. This is how we survive."

Coding Task:

  1. First read (2 minutes): What's happening in this story?
  2. Descriptive coding (4 minutes): Identify 5-7 descriptive codes
  3. Interpretive coding (4 minutes): What deeper patterns do you see?
  4. In-vivo codes (2 minutes): Which phrases capture key concepts?

Pair Discussion (6 minutes): Compare codes and discuss analytical insights

Part 2: Research Ethics in Practice

20 minutes

Navigating Complex Ethical Terrain

‚öñÔ∏è Ethics Beyond Consent Forms

Qualitative research often involves unexpected ethical dilemmas that emerge during fieldwork. Researchers must balance multiple obligations: to participants, to knowledge, to communities, and to broader society.

üåê Contextual Ethics Framework

Ethical Principle South Asian Considerations Practical Challenges Mitigation Strategies
Informed Consent Oral traditions, literacy levels, power hierarchies Family gatekeepers, ongoing consent, understanding research Multi-stage consent, visual aids, community explanation
Confidentiality Small communities, family networks, government surveillance Anonymity impossible, data security, researcher safety Composite profiles, secure storage, exit strategies
Do No Harm Marginalized groups, political sensitivity, economic vulnerability Raising expectations, exposing illegal activities, family conflicts Clear boundaries, resource sharing, ongoing check-ins
Reciprocity Resource disparities, knowledge extraction, capacity building Appropriate compensation, skill sharing, research ownership Participatory methods, training, shared dissemination

üö® Ethical Dilemma Case Study

Situation: During interviews with domestic workers about working conditions, several participants reveal that their employers are withholding wages and confiscating identity documents. Some describe situations that constitute trafficking. They trust you because you've built rapport, but they're afraid of losing their jobs if anything is reported.

Competing Obligations:

  • To Participants: Respect their autonomy and protect their confidentiality
  • To Society: Address serious human rights violations
  • To Knowledge: Document important social problems
  • Legal: Potential mandatory reporting requirements

Additional Complexities:

  • Participants are undocumented immigrants with few legal protections
  • Your research is funded by a government agency
  • Local NGOs have mixed relationships with this community
  • Media attention could help or harm these workers

üõ†Ô∏è Exercise 2: Ethical Decision-Making (15 minutes)

Small Groups (3-4 people, 12 minutes):

Working with the domestic workers case study above, develop an ethical response strategy:

  1. Stakeholder Analysis (3 minutes): Who has stakes in this situation?
  2. Options Assessment (4 minutes): What are your possible courses of action?
  3. Ethical Framework (3 minutes): How do you weigh competing principles?
  4. Action Plan (2 minutes): What would you actually do, step by step?

Report Back (3 minutes): Each group shares their approach and reasoning

Reflection Question: How might your decision change if participants explicitly asked for your help versus if they didn't?

Part 3: Mixed Methods Integration

20 minutes

Combining Qualitative and Quantitative Insights

üîó Mixed Methods Designs

Design Sequence Purpose South Asian Example
Exploratory Sequential QUAL ‚Üí quant Develop survey instruments, identify variables FGDs on sanitation practices ‚Üí large-scale hygiene survey
Explanatory Sequential QUANT ‚Üí qual Explain statistical results, understand mechanisms Education test scores ‚Üí interviews with underperforming students
Convergent Parallel QUAL + QUANT Comprehensive understanding, triangulation Microfinance impact: RCT + ethnographic study
Embedded qual within QUANT or QUANT within qual Support primary method, add depth/breadth Health intervention trial with qualitative process evaluation

üìä Integration Example: Women's Financial Inclusion

Research Question: How does access to formal banking services affect women's economic empowerment in rural India?

Quantitative Component:

  • Design: Panel survey of 2,000 women before/after bank branch opening
  • Measures: Account ownership, savings, credit access, household decision-making
  • Findings: 40% increase in account ownership, 15% increase in savings, mixed results on empowerment

Qualitative Component:

  • Design: Life history interviews with 60 women, FGDs with families
  • Focus: Banking experiences, family dynamics, practical challenges
  • Findings: Account ownership ‚â† account control; informal barriers persist

Integrated Insights:

  • Quantitative data shows formal inclusion progress
  • Qualitative data reveals persistent informal exclusion
  • Together: Physical access is necessary but not sufficient for empowerment
  • Policy implications: Need complementary interventions addressing social norms

Integration Principle: "The whole should be greater than the sum of its parts. Mixed methods integration creates insights that neither approach could generate alone."

Part 4: Writing and Presenting Qualitative Research

15 minutes

From Analysis to Impact

‚úçÔ∏è Writing Strategies for Different Audiences

Audience Key Elements Language Style Evidence Approach
Academic Theory, methodology, contribution to literature Formal, precise, technical terminology Systematic coding examples, thick description
Policy Clear recommendations, implementation guidance Direct, action-oriented, minimal jargon Representative quotes, compelling narratives
Practitioner Practical insights, lessons learned, tools Accessible, experience-based, reflective Case studies, process documentation
Community Validation, shared understanding, collective action Respectful, collaborative, culturally appropriate Participant voices, visual presentations

üìù Quality Criteria for Qualitative Research

  • Credibility: Do findings ring true? Multiple data sources, member checking, prolonged engagement
  • Transferability: Are findings relevant elsewhere? Rich description, clear context, theoretical connections
  • Dependability: Is the process systematic? Audit trail, consistent methods, reflexive documentation
  • Confirmability: Are findings grounded in data? Raw data examples, negative case analysis, researcher reflexivity

üõ†Ô∏è Exercise 4: Research Communication (10 minutes)

Individual Work (7 minutes):

You've completed a study on barriers to girls' education in rural communities. Write three different 100-word summaries of your key finding:

Key Finding: "While economic constraints are commonly cited as the primary barrier to girls' education, our research reveals that safety concerns and social norms around girls' mobility after puberty are actually the decisive factors in families' decisions to withdraw daughters from school."

Write for:

  1. Academic journal (2 minutes): Focus on theoretical implications
  2. Policy brief (2 minutes): Emphasize actionable recommendations
  3. Community report (3 minutes): Center participant voices and local relevance

Peer Review (3 minutes): Exchange summaries with a partner and discuss differences

üåü Key Takeaway

Rigorous qualitative research requires systematic analysis, ethical sensitivity, and strategic communication. The goal is not just to describe what people say, but to understand what their experiences reveal about larger social processes.
