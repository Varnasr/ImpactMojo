DATA FEMINISM
7 Principles
  1. Examine Power - Who has power over data?
  2. Challenge Power - Work toward justice
  3. Elevate Emotion & Embodiment - Value lived experience
  4. Rethink Binaries - Challenge classification systems
  5. Embrace Pluralism - Multiple knowledge systems
  6. Consider Context - Data reflects social relations
  7. Make Labor Visible - Credit all contributors
Remember: Data is never neutral. It reflects power structures and social inequalities.
Catherine D'Ignazio & Lauren Klein, Data Feminism (2020)

KEY CONCEPTS
Data Feminism
Matrix of Domination: How race, class, gender, caste intersect to create overlapping systems of oppression
Situated Knowledge: All knowledge comes from particular perspectives - no "view from nowhere"
Co-liberation: Oppressive systems harm everyone; liberation must be collective

Data Ethics vs Data Justice:

  • Ethics → Focus on bias
  • Justice → Focus on oppression
  • Ethics → Individual solutions
  • Justice → Structural change
Patricia Hill Collins, Donna Haraway, Combahee River Collective

INTERSECTIONAL
ANALYSIS

Multiple Exclusions:

  • Rural women: Invisible in data, excluded from programs, unpaid work
  • Migrant workers: Missing from databases, can't access state programs, no protection
  • Platform workers: Algorithmic discrimination, no social security, misclassified

Intersectional Identities:

  • Caste + Gender + Class
  • Religion + Region + Language
  • Age + Disability + Migration status
Always ask: How do different systems of oppression interact?
No single-issue solutions - think systemically

FIELD RESEARCH
QUESTIONS

Data Feminism Lens:

  • Whose knowledge is valued/ignored?
  • What work is invisible here?
  • How do power structures shape data?

Safety Nets Assessment:

  • What programs exist locally?
  • Who gets access? Who's excluded?
  • How do people cope with shocks?

Decent Work Evaluation:

  • What are working conditions like?
  • How do workers organize/resist?
  • What rights violations occur?
Listen to: Community members' own analysis of problems
Community knowledge > external assumptions
ACTION PLANNING
FRAMEWORK

1. Analyze Root Causes:

  • What systems create the problem?
  • Who benefits from status quo?

2. Map Stakeholders:

  • Who has power to change things?
  • Who are potential allies?

3. Identify Entry Points:

  • Policy windows
  • Organizing opportunities
  • Research gaps

4. Build Coalitions:

  • Center affected communities
  • Connect across issues
Start small: Build power through concrete victories
Systemic change requires collective action
